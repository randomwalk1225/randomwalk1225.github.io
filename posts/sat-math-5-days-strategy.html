The SAT is 5 days away — March 14. If you're reading this, you're either well-prepared and looking for final polish, or you're panicking. Either way, this guide is for you.

The goal this week is not to learn new material. It's to consolidate what you know, eliminate careless mistakes, and build exam-day confidence. Here's exactly how.

📌 Quick summary for the busy student:
Days 5–4: Review your weakest topic (1 topic only). Day 3: Full practice section under timed conditions. Day 2: Error log review + rest. Day 1 (eve): Light review, sleep early. Day 0: Trust your prep.

What the SAT Math Section Actually Tests

The SAT Math section has two modules, each 35 minutes with 22 questions. Module 2 difficulty adapts to your Module 1 performance — do well on Module 1 and you get harder (higher-scoring) questions in Module 2.

Algebra13–15Linear equations, systems, inequalities
Advanced Math13–15Quadratics, functions, exponents
Problem-Solving & Data5–7Ratios, percentages, statistics

5-Day Plan: Focused, Not Frantic

Day 5 (Today) — Identify Your Weak Spot

Look at your most recent practice test. Find the one domain where you lost the most points. That's your focus for today and tomorrow — not everything, just that one area.

Most common weak spots:
• Quadratic word problems (setup errors)
• System of equations with 3 variables
• Interpreting graphs in context
• Right triangle trigonometry (\(\sin\), \(\cos\), \(\tan\) in non-standard orientation)

Day 4 — Targeted Drill (60–90 min max)

Do 10–15 focused problems on that weak area. Don't time yourself. Work carefully, check your setup before calculating. This builds pattern recognition, not speed.

For algebra: practice writing the equation before solving. Most errors are in setup, not computation.

For quadratics: drill the three forms — standard \(ax^2 + bx + c\), vertex \(a(x-h)^2 + k\), and factored \(a(x-r)(x-s)\). Know when to use each.

For geometry: memorize the reference formulas on the exam — but also know when not to use them (many students over-apply the area formulas).

Day 3 — Full Timed Section

Take one complete Math section (both modules, full 70 minutes) under real conditions: phone away, no pausing, calculator use matches your test-day plan.

After: don't just count your score. Categorize your mistakes:

  • Careless errors — you knew how, made an arithmetic mistake → slow down 10 seconds per question
  • Concept gaps — didn't know the approach → review that concept tonight
  • Time pressure — ran out of time → flag harder questions and return, don't get stuck

Day 2 — Error Log + Light Review

Write down every mistake from Day 3 in an error log. One line per mistake: what the question tested, what you did wrong, what the correct approach is. Reading this on the morning of the exam is more valuable than doing 50 more practice problems.

⚠️ What NOT to do on Day 2:
Don't cram new topics. Don't do a full practice test. Don't study past 10pm. Your brain consolidates memory during sleep — rest is part of your prep.

Day 1 (Eve of Exam) — Logistics + Confidence

Read your error log once. Pack your bag: valid photo ID, admission ticket, approved calculator, pencils, snacks. Know exactly how you're getting to the test center and when you need to leave.

Do 5–10 easy warm-up problems to get in math mode. Then stop. Watch something enjoyable. Sleep by 10pm.

The 3 Most Common SAT Math Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

1. Not Reading the Question Carefully

The SAT often asks for something slightly different than what you solve for. "What is the value of \(2x\)?" after you find \(x\). Or "which of the following must be true?" vs "could be true." Read the last sentence of each question twice before answering.

2. Calculator Over-Reliance

Students who rely heavily on their calculator often make input errors that feel invisible. For algebra problems, solve mentally or by hand when possible. Use the calculator to verify, not to find.

3. Skipping the Diagram

For geometry and word problems, always draw a diagram or label the given information visually. Even a quick sketch prevents misreading of relationships between variables.

Key Formulas to Have Ready

The SAT provides a reference sheet, but knowing these cold saves you 15–20 seconds per relevant question:

  • Slope: \(m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\)
  • Quadratic formula: \(x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)
  • Discriminant: if \(b^2 - 4ac > 0\), two real roots; \(= 0\), one; \(< 0\), none
  • Vertex of parabola: \(x = -\dfrac{b}{2a}\)
  • Distance: \(d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}\)
  • Percent change: \(\dfrac{\text{new} - \text{old}}{\text{old}} \times 100\)

On Test Day

Arrive 20 minutes early. During Module 1, work at a steady pace — don't rush, don't linger. Flag any question that takes more than 90 seconds and return to it. Your goal in Module 1 is to get enough right to unlock the harder Module 2 (which has higher ceiling scores).

In Module 2: if you get harder questions, that's good — you're on the high-score path. Don't panic. Work through them methodically.

✅ Final mindset: You've already done most of the work. This week is about not undoing it. Stay calm, execute your process, and trust your preparation.
