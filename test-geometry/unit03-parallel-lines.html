Unit 3 — Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Unit 3 of 13 · Grade 9–10 · Common Core G-CO.1, G-CO.9

Two of the most important relationships between lines are parallelism and perpendicularity. When a third line — a transversal — crosses two parallel lines, it creates a rich pattern of angle pairs that are the basis for countless theorems. In this unit you will identify and prove these angle relationships, learn how to demonstrate that lines are parallel, and connect everything to slope on the coordinate plane.

3.1 Lines and Angles

Key Vocabulary

  • Parallel lines ($\parallel$): coplanar lines that never intersect. Written $\ell \parallel m$.
  • Perpendicular lines ($\perp$): lines that intersect at $90°$. Written $\ell \perp m$.
  • Skew lines: non-coplanar lines that neither intersect nor are parallel (found in 3-D figures).
  • Transversal: a line that intersects two or more other lines at distinct points, creating angle pairs at each intersection.

3.2 Parallel Lines and Transversals

Corresponding Angles Postulate

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then corresponding angles are congruent.

$$\ell \parallel m \implies \angle 1 \cong \angle 5,\quad \angle 2 \cong \angle 6,\quad \angle 3 \cong \angle 7,\quad \angle 4 \cong \angle 8$$

Alternate Interior Angles Theorem

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then alternate interior angles are congruent.

$$\ell \parallel m \implies \angle 3 \cong \angle 6 \text{ and } \angle 4 \cong \angle 5$$

Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then alternate exterior angles are congruent.

$$\ell \parallel m \implies \angle 1 \cong \angle 8 \text{ and } \angle 2 \cong \angle 7$$

Same-Side Interior Angles Theorem (Co-interior / Consecutive Interior)

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then same-side interior angles are supplementary.

$$\ell \parallel m \implies m\angle 3 + m\angle 5 = 180° \text{ and } m\angle 4 + m\angle 6 = 180°$$

Example 3.2 — Finding All Angle Measures

Given: $\ell \parallel m$ and $m\angle 1 = 65°$. Find the measures of $\angle 2$ through $\angle 8$.

Solution:

  • $m\angle 1 = 65°$ (given)
  • $m\angle 2 = 180° - 65° = 115°$ (linear pair with $\angle 1$)
  • $m\angle 3 = 115°$ (vertical angles with $\angle 2$)
  • $m\angle 4 = 65°$ (vertical angles with $\angle 1$)
  • $m\angle 5 = 65°$ (corresponding angles: $\angle 1 \cong \angle 5$)
  • $m\angle 6 = 115°$ (corresponding angles: $\angle 2 \cong \angle 6$)
  • $m\angle 7 = 115°$ (corresponding angles: $\angle 3 \cong \angle 7$)
  • $m\angle 8 = 65°$ (corresponding angles: $\angle 4 \cong \angle 8$)

Check: $m\angle 3 + m\angle 5 = 115° + 65° = 180°$ ✓ (same-side interior)

3.3 Proving Lines Parallel

Five Ways to Prove Lines Are Parallel

To prove $\ell \parallel m$, show that one of the following holds for a transversal cutting the lines:

  1. Converse of Corresponding Angles Postulate: A pair of corresponding angles is congruent.
  2. Converse of Alternate Interior Angles Theorem: A pair of alternate interior angles is congruent.
  3. Converse of Alternate Exterior Angles Theorem: A pair of alternate exterior angles is congruent.
  4. Converse of Same-Side Interior Angles Theorem: A pair of same-side interior angles is supplementary (sums to $180°$).
  5. Two Lines Perpendicular to the Same Line: If $\ell \perp t$ and $m \perp t$, then $\ell \parallel m$.

3.4 Perpendicular Lines

Perpendicular Transversal Theorem

In a plane, if a transversal is perpendicular to one of two parallel lines, then it is perpendicular to the other line as well.

Formally: if $\ell \parallel m$ and $t \perp \ell$, then $t \perp m$.

Distance from a Point to a Line

The distance from a point $P$ to a line $\ell$ is the length of the perpendicular segment from $P$ to $\ell$. This is the shortest distance from the point to any point on the line.

Distance between parallel lines: the perpendicular distance between them, which is constant everywhere along the lines.

3.5 Slopes of Lines

Slope Criteria for Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

In the coordinate plane, the slope of a non-vertical line through $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
  • Parallel lines have equal slopes: $m_1 = m_2$ (and different $y$-intercepts).
  • Perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals: $m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$.

Special cases: every vertical line has undefined slope; a horizontal line has slope $0$. A vertical line is perpendicular to a horizontal line.

Example 3.5a — Identifying Parallel Lines

Question: Are $y = 2x + 3$ and $y = 2x - 1$ parallel or perpendicular?

Solution: Both lines have slope $m = 2$. Since the slopes are equal and the $y$-intercepts differ, the lines are parallel.

Example 3.5b — Finding a Perpendicular Line

Question: Find an equation of the line perpendicular to $y = 3x + 1$ that passes through the origin $(0, 0)$.

Solution: The slope of $y = 3x + 1$ is $3$. The negative reciprocal is $-\dfrac{1}{3}$.

Using slope-intercept form with slope $-\dfrac{1}{3}$ and $y$-intercept $0$:

$$y = -\frac{1}{3}x$$

Check: $3 \cdot \left(-\dfrac{1}{3}\right) = -1$ ✓

  1. Name the relationship (corresponding, alternate interior, alternate exterior, or same-side interior) between $\angle 2$ and $\angle 6$ in Figure 3.1.
  2. In Figure 3.1, $\ell \parallel m$ and $m\angle 4 = 72°$. Find the measures of all eight angles.
  3. $\ell \parallel m$. $\angle 3 = (4x + 15)°$ and $\angle 6 = (7x - 12)°$. Find $x$ and the measures of $\angle 3$ and $\angle 6$. (Hint: alternate interior angles)
  4. $\ell \parallel m$. $\angle 4 = (5x + 10)°$ and $\angle 5 = (3x + 30)°$. Find $x$. (Hint: same-side interior → supplementary)
  5. Two lines are cut by a transversal. A pair of corresponding angles measures $58°$ and $(3x - 2)°$. What value of $x$ makes the lines parallel?
  6. Name two ways to prove $\ell \parallel m$ without using corresponding angles.
  7. Line $t$ is perpendicular to line $\ell$. Line $\ell \parallel m$. What is the relationship between $t$ and $m$? Justify.
  8. Find the slope of a line parallel to the line through $(1, 3)$ and $(4, 9)$.
  9. Line $\ell$ has slope $\dfrac{2}{5}$. What is the slope of any line perpendicular to $\ell$?
  10. Are the lines $3x + y = 7$ and $x - 3y = 2$ parallel, perpendicular, or neither? Show your work.
  11. Write the equation of the line perpendicular to $y = -\dfrac{1}{2}x + 4$ that passes through $(2, 3)$.
  12. Write the equation of the line parallel to $2x - y = 5$ passing through $(-1, 4)$.
