Unit 4 — Congruent Triangles

Unit 4 of 13 · Grade 9–10 · Common Core G-CO.6–8

Triangles are the simplest polygon and the foundation of all geometric reasoning about shapes. When two triangles are congruent, every corresponding side and angle matches exactly. In this unit you will classify triangles by their sides and angles, apply the Triangle Sum Theorem, identify the five triangle congruence postulates and theorems, write formal proofs, and explore special properties of isosceles and equilateral triangles.

4.1 Triangle Classification

Classifying Triangles

By side lengths:

  • Scalene: no two sides are congruent.
  • Isosceles: at least two sides are congruent (the equal sides are the legs; the third side is the base).
  • Equilateral: all three sides are congruent.

By angle measures:

  • Acute: all three angles are acute ($< 90°$).
  • Right: exactly one angle is a right angle ($= 90°$). The side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse.
  • Obtuse: exactly one angle is obtuse ($> 90°$).
  • Equiangular: all three angles are congruent (each measures $60°$). An equiangular triangle is also equilateral.

4.2 Triangle Sum Theorem

Triangle Sum Theorem

The sum of the interior angle measures of any triangle is $180°$:

$$m\angle A + m\angle B + m\angle C = 180°$$

Exterior Angle Theorem

An exterior angle of a triangle is formed by one side and the extension of an adjacent side. It equals the sum of the two non-adjacent (remote) interior angles:

$$m\angle \text{ext} = m\angle A + m\angle B$$

Example 4.2 — Finding a Missing Angle

Given: A triangle has two angles measuring $45°$ and $70°$. Find the third angle.

Solution:

$$m\angle C = 180° - 45° - 70° = 65°$$

4.3 Congruence and Triangles

Congruent Figures and CPCTC

Two figures are congruent if they have the same shape and size — one can be mapped onto the other by a rigid motion (rotation, reflection, or translation).

For triangles, the notation $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$ means the vertices correspond in order: $A \leftrightarrow D$, $B \leftrightarrow E$, $C \leftrightarrow F$. This implies:

  • $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{DE}$, $\overline{BC} \cong \overline{EF}$, $\overline{AC} \cong \overline{DF}$ (corresponding sides)
  • $\angle A \cong \angle D$, $\angle B \cong \angle E$, $\angle C \cong \angle F$ (corresponding angles)

CPCTC — Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles are Congruent. Once two triangles are proved congruent, all six pairs of corresponding parts are automatically congruent. CPCTC is used as the final reason in many proofs.

Third Angles Theorem

If two angles of one triangle are congruent to two angles of another triangle, then the third angles are also congruent.

4.4 Triangle Congruence Postulates and Theorems

To prove two triangles congruent it is not necessary to show all six pairs of corresponding parts. The following five shortcuts suffice:

SSA and AAA Do NOT Guarantee Congruence!

  • SSA (Side-Side-Angle) — two sides and a non-included angle do not determine a unique triangle. Two different triangles may satisfy the same SSA conditions (the "ambiguous case").
  • AAA (Angle-Angle-Angle) — three congruent angle pairs only guarantees similar (same shape) triangles, not congruent (same size) ones.

The only exception: HL uses an angle (the right angle) plus the two sides that form the Pythagorean relationship, which resolves the ambiguity.

4.6 Isosceles and Equilateral Triangles

Base Angles Theorem (Isosceles Triangle Theorem)

If two sides of a triangle are congruent, then the angles opposite those sides are congruent.

In $\triangle ABC$ with $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{AC}$: $\angle B \cong \angle C$.

Converse of the Base Angles Theorem

If two angles of a triangle are congruent, then the sides opposite those angles are congruent.

In $\triangle ABC$ with $\angle B \cong \angle C$: $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{AC}$.

Equilateral Triangle Corollary

An equilateral triangle is also equiangular — each interior angle measures exactly $60°$.

Conversely, if all three angles of a triangle are congruent, the triangle is equilateral.

Example 4.6 — Isosceles Triangle Angles

Given: $\triangle PQR$ is isosceles with $\overline{PQ} \cong \overline{PR}$. The vertex angle $m\angle P = 40°$. Find the base angles.

Solution: The two base angles $\angle Q$ and $\angle R$ are congruent (Base Angles Theorem). Let each measure $x$.

$$40° + x + x = 180°$$ $$2x = 140°$$ $$x = 70°$$

So $m\angle Q = m\angle R = 70°$.

Practice Problems

Unit 4 Practice — 12 Problems

  1. Classify each triangle by sides and by angles: (a) sides $5, 5, 5$ (b) sides $3, 4, 5$ with a $90°$ angle (c) angles $30°, 60°, 90°$ (d) angles $110°, 40°, 30°$.
    Show Solution

    (a) All sides equal → equilateral (by sides); all angles $60°$ → equiangular/acute (by angles).

    (b) No two sides equal → scalene; one $90°$ angle → right scalene.

    (c) No two angles equal → scalene; one $90°$ angle → right scalene.

    (d) No two angles equal → scalene; one angle $> 90°$ → obtuse scalene.

  2. The angles of a triangle are $(2x + 5)°$, $(x + 10)°$, and $(3x - 15)°$. Find $x$ and the measure of each angle.
    Show Solution

    $(2x+5) + (x+10) + (3x-15) = 180$

    $6x = 180 \Rightarrow x = 30$.

    Angles: $\mathbf{65°}$, $\mathbf{40°}$, $\mathbf{75°}$.

  3. An exterior angle of a triangle measures $118°$. One non-adjacent interior angle is $47°$. Find the other non-adjacent interior angle.
    Show Solution

    Exterior Angle Theorem: exterior angle = sum of two remote interior angles.

    $118° = 47° + x \Rightarrow x = \mathbf{71°}$.

  4. $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle XYZ$. $AB = 12$, $m\angle B = 54°$, $m\angle C = 63°$. Find $XY$, $m\angle Y$, and $m\angle Z$.
    Show Solution

    Corresponding parts: $A \leftrightarrow X$, $B \leftrightarrow Y$, $C \leftrightarrow Z$.

    $XY = AB = \mathbf{12}$; $m\angle Y = m\angle B = \mathbf{54°}$; $m\angle Z = m\angle C = \mathbf{63°}$.

  5. Name the congruence postulate or theorem (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, or HL) that proves the triangles congruent, or state "not enough information":
    (a) All three sides of $\triangle PQR$ are marked congruent to corresponding sides of $\triangle STU$.
    (b) Two angles and the included side of $\triangle ABC$ match those of $\triangle DEF$.
    (c) Two sides of $\triangle JKL$ equal two sides of $\triangle MNO$, with a non-included angle matching.
    (d) Right triangles $\triangle GHI$ and $\triangle JKL$ share a congruent hypotenuse and one leg.
    Show Solution

    (a) SSS — three pairs of congruent sides.

    (b) ASA — two angles and the included side.

    (c) Not enough information — SSA is the ambiguous case.

    (d) HL — right triangles with congruent hypotenuse and leg.

  6. Why can't AAA prove triangle congruence? Give an example of two non-congruent triangles that are equiangular.
    Show Solution

    AAA only guarantees that triangles are similar (same shape), not congruent (same size). The sides can scale independently.

    Example: an equilateral triangle with side $3$ and one with side $6$ both have all angles $60°$, yet they are not congruent.

  7. Proof: Given $\overline{AB} \parallel \overline{CD}$ and $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$, prove $\triangle ABE \cong \triangle DCE$ where $E$ is the intersection of $\overline{AC}$ and $\overline{BD}$. (Hint: use Alternate Interior Angles, then ASA.)
    Show Solution

    1. $\overline{AB} \parallel \overline{CD}$ — Given.

    2. $\angle ABE \cong \angle DCE$ — Alternate Interior Angles Theorem.

    3. $\angle BAE \cong \angle CDE$ — Alternate Interior Angles Theorem.

    4. $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$ — Given.

    5. $\triangle ABE \cong \triangle DCE$ — ASA Congruence Postulate (steps 2, 4, 3). $\blacksquare$

  8. $\triangle RST$ is isosceles with $RS = RT = 15$ and base $ST = 18$. Find $m\angle S$ and $m\angle T$ if the vertex angle $m\angle R = 56°$.
    Show Solution

    Base angles are congruent (Base Angles Theorem). Let $m\angle S = m\angle T = x$.

    $56° + x + x = 180° \Rightarrow 2x = 124° \Rightarrow x = 62°$.

    $m\angle S = m\angle T = \mathbf{62°}$.

  9. In equilateral $\triangle ABC$, $AB = 3x - 6$ and $BC = x + 10$. Find the side length.
    Show Solution

    All sides equal: $3x - 6 = x + 10$.

    $2x = 16 \Rightarrow x = 8$.

    Side length $= x + 10 = \mathbf{18}$.

  10. $\triangle KLM$ is isosceles with $\angle K \cong \angle M$. $m\angle K = (4x + 8)°$ and $m\angle L = (2x + 20)°$. Find all three angle measures.
    Show Solution

    $m\angle K + m\angle L + m\angle M = 180°$, with $m\angle K = m\angle M = (4x+8)°$.

    $2(4x+8) + (2x+20) = 180 \Rightarrow 10x + 36 = 180 \Rightarrow x = 14.4$.

    $m\angle K = m\angle M = \mathbf{65.6°}$; $m\angle L = \mathbf{48.8°}$.

  11. CPCTC Proof: Given $\overline{WX} \cong \overline{YZ}$ and $\overline{WZ} \cong \overline{YX}$, prove $\angle W \cong \angle Y$ using diagonal $\overline{WY}$ of quadrilateral $WXYZ$.
    Show Solution

    1. Draw $\overline{WY}$ — Construction.

    2. $\overline{WX} \cong \overline{YZ}$, $\overline{WZ} \cong \overline{YX}$ — Given.

    3. $\overline{WY} \cong \overline{WY}$ — Reflexive Property.

    4. $\triangle WXY \cong \triangle YZW$ — SSS Congruence Postulate.

    5. $\angle W \cong \angle Y$ — CPCTC. $\blacksquare$

  12. The vertex angle of an isosceles triangle is three times each base angle. Find all three angle measures.
    Show Solution

    Let each base angle $= x$. Then vertex angle $= 3x$.

    $3x + x + x = 180° \Rightarrow 5x = 180° \Rightarrow x = 36°$.

    Base angles: $\mathbf{36°}$ each. Vertex angle: $\mathbf{108°}$.
