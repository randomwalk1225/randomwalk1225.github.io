Triangle Congruence: SSS, SAS, and ASA

Geometry • High School • MathHub Global • February 2026

Two triangles are congruent when they have exactly the same shape and size ‚Äî one can be placed perfectly on top of the other (by flipping, rotating, or sliding). In this lesson you will learn three powerful shortcuts for proving triangle congruence without measuring every single part.

What Does Congruence Mean?

Definition ‚Äî Congruent Triangles

$\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$ means there is a correspondence between vertices such that all six parts match:

  • Three pairs of congruent sides: $AB=DE,\; BC=EF,\; CA=FD$
  • Three pairs of congruent angles: $\angle A=\angle D,\; \angle B=\angle E,\; \angle C=\angle F$

Checking all six parts is tedious. Fortunately, just three of them (chosen carefully) are enough. That gives us the three congruence postulates below.

1. SSS ‚Äî Side-Side-Side

SSS Congruence Postulate

If three sides of one triangle are congruent to three sides of another triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

$$\overline{AB}\cong\overline{DE},\;\overline{BC}\cong\overline{EF},\;\overline{CA}\cong\overline{FD} \;\Longrightarrow\; \triangle ABC\cong\triangle DEF$$

üí° Why SSS Works

Think of a triangle as a rigid frame. Once all three side lengths are fixed, there is only one possible shape ‚Äî the frame cannot flex. This rigidity is what makes SSS a valid proof method.

Example 1 ‚Äî SSS

Given: $AB = 5$, $BC = 7$, $CA = 6$, $DE = 5$, $EF = 7$, $FD = 6$.

Prove: $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$

  1. $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{DE}$ (both = 5)  ‚úì
  2. $\overline{BC} \cong \overline{EF}$ (both = 7)  ‚úì
  3. $\overline{CA} \cong \overline{FD}$ (both = 6)  ‚úì
  4. $\therefore\; \triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$ by SSS

2. SAS ‚Äî Side-Angle-Side

SAS Congruence Postulate

If two sides and the included angle (the angle between those two sides) of one triangle are congruent to the corresponding parts of another triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

$$\overline{AB}\cong\overline{DE},\;\angle B\cong\angle E,\;\overline{BC}\cong\overline{EF} \;\Longrightarrow\; \triangle ABC\cong\triangle DEF$$

‚ö†Ô∏è The "Included" Angle Matters

The angle must be between the two sides. SSA (side-side-angle) is NOT a valid congruence rule ‚Äî two different triangles can share the same two sides and a non-included angle.

Example 2 ‚Äî SAS

Given: $\overline{KL}\cong\overline{NP}$, $\overline{LM}\cong\overline{PQ}$, $\angle L \cong \angle P$.

  1. $\overline{KL}\cong\overline{NP}$  (given) ‚úì
  2. $\angle L \cong \angle P$  (included angle, given) ‚úì
  3. $\overline{LM}\cong\overline{PQ}$  (given) ‚úì
  4. $\therefore\; \triangle KLM \cong \triangle NPQ$ by SAS

3. ASA ‚Äî Angle-Side-Angle

ASA Congruence Postulate

If two angles and the included side (the side between those two angles) of one triangle are congruent to the corresponding parts of another triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

$$\angle A\cong\angle D,\;\overline{AB}\cong\overline{DE},\;\angle B\cong\angle E \;\Longrightarrow\; \triangle ABC\cong\triangle DEF$$

üí° ASA vs AAS

In ASA, the side is between the two angles. In AAS (Angle-Angle-Side), the side is not between them. Both are valid ‚Äî but you must label them correctly in a proof.

Example 3 ‚Äî ASA

Given: $\angle R \cong \angle U$, $\overline{RS} \cong \overline{UV}$, $\angle S \cong \angle V$.

  1. $\angle R \cong \angle U$  (given) ‚úì
  2. $\overline{RS} \cong \overline{UV}$  (included side, given) ‚úì
  3. $\angle S \cong \angle V$  (given) ‚úì
  4. $\therefore\; \triangle RST \cong \triangle UVW$ by ASA

Summary ‚Äî Which Postulate to Use?

Postulate What You Need Key Condition
SSS 3 pairs of congruent sides All three sides match
SAS 2 sides + included angle Angle is between the two sides
ASA 2 angles + included side Side is between the two angles
SSA ‚úó 2 sides + non-included angle NOT valid ‚Äî ambiguous case
AAA ‚úó 3 pairs of congruent angles NOT valid ‚Äî only proves similarity

Essential Examples

Work through these carefully before attempting the exercises. Each one highlights a different reasoning skill.

Essential Example A ‚Äî Find the Missing Value (SSS)

Two triangles share the relationship $\triangle PQR \cong \triangle STU$. You are given $PQ = 3x - 2$, $ST = 19$, $QR = 14$, $TU = 14$, and $PR = 2y + 1$, $SU = 13$.

Find $x$ and $y$.

Show Full Solution

Step 1: Because $\triangle PQR \cong \triangle STU$, corresponding sides are equal.

Step 2: $PQ$ corresponds to $ST$:

$3x - 2 = 19 \;\Rightarrow\; 3x = 21 \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{x = 7}$

Step 3: $PR$ corresponds to $SU$:

$2y + 1 = 13 \;\Rightarrow\; 2y = 12 \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{y = 6}$

Check: $PQ = 3(7)-2 = 19$ ‚úì, $PR = 2(6)+1 = 13$ ‚úì

Essential Example B ‚Äî Identify from a Diagram Description (SAS)

In quadrilateral $ABCD$, diagonal $\overline{AC}$ is drawn. You know $\overline{AB}\cong\overline{CB}$ and $\overline{AD}\cong\overline{CD}$. A classmate claims $\triangle ABD \cong \triangle CBD$ by SSS. Is she correct? If not, what postulate does apply?

Show Full Solution

Given: $AB\cong CB$, $AD\cong CD$, and $\overline{BD}\cong\overline{BD}$ (reflexive property ‚Äî shared side).

That is three pairs of congruent sides, so the classmate is correct: $\triangle ABD \cong \triangle CBD$ by SSS.

Note: The reflexive property is a key tool ‚Äî a segment shared by two triangles is automatically congruent to itself.

Essential Example C ‚Äî Write a Two-Column Proof (ASA)

Given: $\overline{AB} \parallel \overline{CD}$ and $M$ is the midpoint of $\overline{BC}$.
Prove: $\triangle ABM \cong \triangle DCM$.

Show Full Solution
StatementReason
1. $\overline{AB}\parallel\overline{CD}$Given
2. $\angle ABM \cong \angle DCM$Alternate interior angles (parallel lines cut by transversal $\overline{BC}$)
3. $M$ is the midpoint of $\overline{BC}$Given
4. $\overline{BM}\cong\overline{CM}$Definition of midpoint
5. $\angle AMB \cong \angle DMC$Vertical angles theorem
6. $\triangle ABM \cong \triangle DCM$ASA (steps 2, 4, 5)

Essential Example D ‚Äî CPCTC (Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles are Congruent)

Given $\triangle XYZ \cong \triangle LMN$, find $m\angle N$ if $m\angle X = 48¬∞$ and $m\angle Y = 73¬∞$.

Show Full Solution

Step 1: Find $m\angle Z$ in $\triangle XYZ$.

$m\angle Z = 180¬∞ - 48¬∞ - 73¬∞ = 59¬∞$

Step 2: By CPCTC, corresponding angles are congruent. $Z$ corresponds to $N$.

$\boxed{m\angle N = 59¬∞}$

Essential Example E ‚Äî Not Enough Information?

Can you prove $\triangle GHI \cong \triangle JKL$ from these facts alone? $GH = JK = 8$, $\angle H = \angle K = 50¬∞$, $HI = KL = 11$.

What if you also learn that $\angle G = \angle J = 60¬∞$?

Show Full Solution

Part 1: $GH\cong JK$ (side), $\angle H\cong\angle K$ (angle), $HI\cong KL$ (side). The angle $\angle H$ is between sides $GH$ and $HI$. This is SAS ‚Äî yes, the triangles are congruent.

Part 2: With the additional fact $\angle G\cong\angle J$, we also have ASA ($\angle G$, $GH$, $\angle H$) or AAS as alternate proofs. Multiple valid paths exist.

Key takeaway: Always check whether the angle is the included angle before claiming SAS.

Answer
ASA ‚Äî $\angle P\cong\angle T$, included side $\overline{PQ}\cong\overline{TU}$ (2 ticks), $\angle Q\cong\angle U$. $\triangle PQR \cong \triangle TUV$.

Identify the postulate from the diagram and write the congruence statement.

Answer
SAS ‚Äî $\overline{GH}\cong\overline{MN}$ (1 tick), $\angle H\cong\angle N$ (included angle), $\overline{HI}\cong\overline{NP}$ (2 ticks). $\triangle GHI \cong \triangle MNP$.
4.

The diagram marks two sides and a non-included angle (at $A$, not between $AB$ and $BC$). Can you prove congruence? Explain.

Answer
No. SSA is not valid. The diagram shows that two different triangles (‚ñ≥ABC and ‚ñ≥AB'C, solid vs dashed) share the same two sides and non-included angle. This is the "ambiguous case." More information is needed to conclude congruence.
5.

$\triangle JKL \cong \triangle PQR$. If $JK = 4x + 3$ and $PQ = 27$, find $x$.

Answer
$4x + 3 = 27 \Rightarrow 4x = 24 \Rightarrow$ $x = 6$.
6.

$\triangle ABC \cong \triangle XYZ$. $m\angle A = 52¬∞$, $m\angle B = 81¬∞$. Find $m\angle Z$.

Answer
$m\angle C = 180¬∞ - 52¬∞ - 81¬∞ = 47¬∞$. Since $C\leftrightarrow Z$ by CPCTC, $m\angle Z = 47¬∞$.
7.

$RS = VW = 13$, $ST = UV = 9$, $RT = UW = 15$. Does SSS apply? Write the correct congruence statement. (Watch the vertex order!)

Answer
Yes, SSS. Match ticks: $RS\cong WV$ (1 tick), $ST\cong VU$ (2 ticks), $RT\cong WU$ (3 ticks). Correspondence: $R\leftrightarrow W$, $S\leftrightarrow V$, $T\leftrightarrow U$. $\triangle RST \cong \triangle WVU$.
8.

$\overline{AC}$ bisects $\angle BAD$ and $\angle BCD$ in quadrilateral $ABCD$. Prove $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle ADC$ and name the postulate. (Hint: the diagonal is shared.)

Answer
$\overline{AC}\cong\overline{AC}$ (reflexive ‚Äî 3 ticks). $\angle BAC\cong\angle DAC$ (AC bisects $\angle BAD$). $\angle BCA\cong\angle DCA$ (AC bisects $\angle BCD$). $\therefore\triangle ABC\cong\triangle ADC$ by ASA.
9.

$\triangle DEF \cong \triangle GHI$. $DE = 3y - 5$, $GH = y + 7$. Find the length of $\overline{DE}$.

Answer
$3y - 5 = y + 7 \Rightarrow 2y = 12 \Rightarrow y = 6$. $DE = 3(6)-5 = \mathbf{13}$.
10.

$M$ is the midpoint of both $\overline{AC}$ and $\overline{BD}$. Name the postulate that proves $\triangle ABM \cong \triangle CDM$ and explain each condition.

Answer
$\overline{AM}\cong\overline{CM}$ and $\overline{BM}\cong\overline{DM}$ (midpoint definition). $\angle AMB\cong\angle CMD$ (vertical angles ‚Äî included). SAS: $\triangle ABM\cong\triangle CDM$.
11.

Can three angles alone prove congruence? $\angle A=\angle D=60¬∞$, $\angle B=\angle E=70¬∞$, $\angle C=\angle F=50¬∞$. Explain.

Answer
No. AAA proves only similarity, not congruence. The triangles could be different sizes ‚Äî an equilateral triangle with side 3 and one with side 10 both have all $60¬∞$ angles.
12.

$\triangle PQR \cong \triangle STU$. $m\angle P = (5x+10)¬∞$ and $m\angle S = (7x-4)¬∞$. Find $m\angle P$.

Answer
$5x+10 = 7x-4 \Rightarrow 14 = 2x \Rightarrow x = 7$. $m\angle P = 5(7)+10 = \mathbf{45¬∞}$.
13.

$\overline{WX}\cong\overline{YZ}$ and $\overline{XY}\cong\overline{ZW}$ in quadrilateral $WXYZ$. Explain why SSS applies when diagonal $\overline{WY}$ is drawn, and write the congruence statement.

Answer
Diagonal $\overline{WY}\cong\overline{WY}$ (reflexive, 3 ticks). With $WX\cong YZ$ (1 tick) and $XY\cong ZW$ (2 ticks): SSS: $\triangle WXY\cong\triangle YZW$.
14.

Are the two triangles congruent? State the postulate if they are.

Answer
Yes, SAS. $AB\cong DE = 10$, $\angle B\cong\angle E = 38¬∞$ (included), $BC\cong EF = 14$. $\therefore \triangle ABC\cong\triangle DEF$.
15.

$\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$, $AB = 2a+3$, $DE = 4a-7$. Also, $BC = 5b-2$, $EF = 3b+8$. Find $a$ and $b$.

Answer
$2a+3=4a-7 \Rightarrow 10=2a \Rightarrow \mathbf{a=5}$.   $5b-2=3b+8 \Rightarrow 2b=10 \Rightarrow \mathbf{b=5}$.
16.

$\overline{AB}\parallel\overline{DC}$ and $\overline{AB}\cong\overline{DC}$. $E$ is where diagonals $\overline{AC}$ and $\overline{BD}$ cross. Prove $\triangle ABE\cong\triangle DCE$ and name the postulate.

Answer
$\angle ABE\cong\angle DCE$ and $\angle BAE\cong\angle CDE$ (alternate interior angles, $AB\parallel DC$). $\overline{AB}\cong\overline{DC}$ (given). ASA: $\triangle ABE\cong\triangle DCE$.
17.

$\triangle KLM \cong \triangle NPQ$. $KL = 18$, $LM = 22$, $KM = 25$. List all six congruence statements (3 sides, 3 angles).

Answer
Sides: $KL\cong NP = 18$; $LM\cong PQ = 22$; $KM\cong NQ = 25$.
Angles: $\angle K\cong\angle N$; $\angle L\cong\angle P$; $\angle M\cong\angle Q$.
18.

Two right triangles each have hypotenuse $= 17$ and one leg $= 8$. Are they congruent? Which postulate applies?

Answer
Yes. Missing leg $= \sqrt{17^2 - 8^2} = \sqrt{225} = 15$. All three sides match (8, 15, 17): SSS. Also fits the HL (Hypotenuse-Leg) theorem for right triangles.
19.

$\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$. $m\angle A = (3x+12)¬∞$, $m\angle D = (5x-8)¬∞$, and $m\angle B = (2y+5)¬∞$, $m\angle E = (4y-11)¬∞$. Find $m\angle A$ and $m\angle B$.

Answer
$\angle A\leftrightarrow\angle D$: $3x+12=5x-8 \Rightarrow x=10$. $m\angle A = \mathbf{42¬∞}$.
$\angle B\leftrightarrow\angle E$: $2y+5=4y-11 \Rightarrow y=8$. $m\angle B = \mathbf{21¬∞}$.
