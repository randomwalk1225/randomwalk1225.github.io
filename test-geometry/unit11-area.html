11.1 Parallelograms and Triangles

Theorem — Area of a Parallelogram and Triangle

Parallelogram: $A = bh$, where $b$ is any base and $h$ is the perpendicular height (altitude) to that base. Note: $h$ is NOT a side length unless the parallelogram is a rectangle.

Triangle: $A = \dfrac{1}{2}bh$ — a triangle has half the area of the parallelogram with the same base and height.

Example — Triangle Area

A triangle has base $10$ and height $6$.

$A = \dfrac{1}{2}(10)(6) = \dfrac{1}{2}(60) = \mathbf{30}$ square units

11.2 Trapezoids, Rhombuses, and Kites

Example — Trapezoid Area

Trapezoid with $b_1 = 5$, $b_2 = 9$, height $h = 4$.

$A = \dfrac{1}{2}(b_1 + b_2)h = \dfrac{1}{2}(5 + 9)(4) = \dfrac{1}{2}(14)(4) = \mathbf{28}$ square units

Example — Rhombus Area

A rhombus has diagonals of length $8$ and $12$.

$A = \dfrac{1}{2}d_1 d_2 = \dfrac{1}{2}(8)(12) = \mathbf{48}$ square units

Note: This formula also applies to kites, since both shapes have perpendicular diagonals.

11.3 Regular Polygons

Definition — Apothem

The apothem of a regular polygon is the perpendicular distance from the center of the polygon to the midpoint of any side. It is the "inradius" of the polygon.

The area formula uses the apothem because the polygon can be divided into congruent triangles, each with base = one side and height = apothem.

$$A = \frac{1}{2} \times a \times P$$

where $a$ = apothem and $P$ = perimeter of the polygon.

Example — Regular Hexagon Area

A regular hexagon with side length $s = 6$.

The apothem of a regular hexagon $= \dfrac{s\sqrt{3}}{2} = \dfrac{6\sqrt{3}}{2} = 3\sqrt{3}$.

Perimeter $= 6 \times 6 = 36$.

$A = \dfrac{1}{2}(3\sqrt{3})(36) = \dfrac{1}{2}(108\sqrt{3}) = 54\sqrt{3} \approx \mathbf{93.5}$ square units

11.4 Circles and Sectors

Example — Circle Area

A circle with radius $r = 7$.

$A = \pi r^2 = \pi (7)^2 = 49\pi \approx \mathbf{153.9}$ square units

Example — Sector Area

A sector with radius $r = 10$ and central angle $\theta = 90°$.

A $90°$ sector is one-quarter of the full circle.

$A = \dfrac{90}{360}\pi(10)^2 = \dfrac{1}{4}(100\pi) = 25\pi \approx \mathbf{78.5}$ square units

11.5 Arc Length

Theorem — Arc Length Formula

Arc length is the actual distance along the circle's circumference for a given central angle $\theta$:

$$\text{Arc Length} = \frac{\theta}{360°} \cdot 2\pi r$$

This is the fraction of the circle's circumference $C = 2\pi r$ corresponding to the arc's central angle.

Example — Finding Arc Length

Circle with $r = 12$ and central angle $\theta = 60°$.

$\text{Arc} = \dfrac{60}{360} \cdot 2\pi(12) = \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot 24\pi = 4\pi \approx \mathbf{12.6}$ units

11.6 Geometric Probability

Definition — Geometric Probability

Geometric probability uses lengths or areas to find the probability of a random event when all points in a region are equally likely to be chosen:

$$P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{favorable length or area}}{\text{total length or area}}$$

Example — Point in Circle vs. Square

A point is chosen at random inside a $10 \times 10$ square. A circle with radius $r = 3$ is inscribed inside the square. Find the probability the point lands inside the circle.

Area of square $= 10 \times 10 = 100$

Area of circle $= \pi(3)^2 = 9\pi$

$P(\text{in circle}) = \dfrac{9\pi}{100} \approx \dfrac{28.27}{100} \approx \mathbf{0.283}$

Practice Problems

Unit 11 Practice — 12 Problems

  1. A parallelogram has base $14$ and height $9$. Find the area.
  2. A triangle has base $18$ and height $11$. Find the area.
  3. A trapezoid has bases $7$ and $13$ and height $6$. Find the area.
  4. A kite has diagonals of length $10$ and $16$. Find the area.
  5. A rhombus has diagonals of length $20$ and $24$. Find the area.
  6. A regular octagon ($n = 8$) has side length $4$ and apothem $\approx 4.83$. Find the area to the nearest tenth.
  7. Find the area of a circle with diameter $18$.
  8. A sector has radius $8$ and central angle $135°$. Find the sector area in terms of $\pi$.
  9. Find the arc length of a sector with radius $15$ and central angle $120°$. Give an exact answer.
  10. A rectangle measures $20 \times 30$. A circle of radius $6$ sits inside it. A point is chosen at random. Find the probability that the point lands outside the circle.
  11. A sector has area $16\pi$ and radius $8$. Find the central angle.
  12. Two concentric circles have radii $3$ and $7$. A point is chosen at random in the larger circle. Find the probability that it lands in the ring between the two circles.
