Unit 2 — Reasoning and Proof

Unit 2 of 13 · Grade 9–10 · Common Core G-CO

Mathematics is distinguished by the certainty of its conclusions — and that certainty comes from proof. In this unit you will develop the logical reasoning skills that make proof possible. You will learn to recognize patterns and form conjectures (inductive reasoning), to chain definitions and postulates into airtight arguments (deductive reasoning), and to write both algebraic and two-column geometric proofs. These skills will be used in every subsequent unit.

2.1 Inductive Reasoning

Inductive Reasoning and Conjectures

Inductive reasoning is the process of observing specific cases, identifying a pattern, and forming a general conclusion called a conjecture.

A conjecture can be disproved by finding a single counterexample — one case where the conjecture fails. A counterexample does not need to be typical; it just needs to exist.

Example 2.1 — Finding a Pattern

Sequence: $2,\; 4,\; 8,\; 16,\; \underline{\phantom{00}}$

Pattern observed: Each term is double the previous term.

Conjecture: The next term is $32$. In general, the $n$-th term is $2^n$.

Counterexample approach: Suppose someone conjectures "the square of every odd number is odd." To disprove it you would need an odd number whose square is even — but no such number exists, so the conjecture is actually true (and must be proved by deduction to be certain).

2.2 Conditional Statements

If-Then Statements and Related Forms

A conditional statement has the form "If $p$, then $q$", written $p \Rightarrow q$, where $p$ is the hypothesis and $q$ is the conclusion.

FormSymbolicWords
Conditional$p \Rightarrow q$If $p$, then $q$.
Converse$q \Rightarrow p$If $q$, then $p$.
Inverse$\neg p \Rightarrow \neg q$If not $p$, then not $q$.
Contrapositive$\neg q \Rightarrow \neg p$If not $q$, then not $p$.

A conditional and its contrapositive always have the same truth value. The converse and inverse are logically equivalent to each other but may differ from the original.

When a conditional and its converse are both true, the result is a biconditional: "$p$ if and only if $q$" ($p \Leftrightarrow q$).

Example 2.2 — Writing Related Statements

Original conditional: "If a triangle has a right angle, then it is a right triangle."

  • Converse: If a triangle is a right triangle, then it has a right angle. (True)
  • Inverse: If a triangle does not have a right angle, then it is not a right triangle. (True)
  • Contrapositive: If a triangle is not a right triangle, then it does not have a right angle. (True)

Since both the conditional and converse are true, the biconditional holds: "A triangle is a right triangle if and only if it has a right angle."

2.3 Deductive Reasoning

Law of Detachment

If $p \Rightarrow q$ is a true conditional and $p$ is true, then $q$ must be true.

Example: "If it is raining, then the ground is wet" (true) + "It is raining" (true) → "The ground is wet."

Law of Syllogism

If $p \Rightarrow q$ and $q \Rightarrow r$ are both true conditionals, then $p \Rightarrow r$ is also true.

Example 2.3 — Law of Syllogism

Statement 1: If a figure is a square, then it is a rectangle. ($p \Rightarrow q$)

Statement 2: If a figure is a rectangle, then it has four right angles. ($q \Rightarrow r$)

Conclusion: If a figure is a square, then it has four right angles. ($p \Rightarrow r$)

2.4 Properties and Postulates

Properties of Equality (for real numbers $a$, $b$, $c$)

  • Reflexive: $a = a$
  • Symmetric: If $a = b$ then $b = a$
  • Transitive: If $a = b$ and $b = c$ then $a = c$
  • Addition: If $a = b$ then $a + c = b + c$
  • Subtraction: If $a = b$ then $a - c = b - c$
  • Multiplication: If $a = b$ then $ac = bc$
  • Division: If $a = b$ and $c \neq 0$ then $\dfrac{a}{c} = \dfrac{b}{c}$
  • Substitution: If $a = b$, then $a$ may replace $b$ in any expression.

Properties of Congruence (for segments or angles)

  • Reflexive: $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{AB}$
  • Symmetric: If $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$ then $\overline{CD} \cong \overline{AB}$
  • Transitive: If $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$ and $\overline{CD} \cong \overline{EF}$ then $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{EF}$

2.5 Algebraic and Two-Column Proofs

A two-column proof organizes logical steps into two columns: the left column lists numbered Statements and the right column gives the Reason (definition, postulate, theorem, or property) that justifies each statement.

Example 2.5a — Algebraic Two-Column Proof

Given: $2x + 4 = 10$. Prove: $x = 3$.

StatementReason
1. $2x + 4 = 10$Given
2. $2x + 4 - 4 = 10 - 4$Subtraction Property of Equality
3. $2x = 6$Simplify
4. $\dfrac{2x}{2} = \dfrac{6}{2}$Division Property of Equality
5. $x = 3$Simplify

Example 2.5b — Geometric Two-Column Proof

Given: Lines $\ell$ and $m$ intersect at $P$, forming $\angle 1$ and $\angle 3$ as vertical angles and $\angle 2$ and $\angle 4$ as vertical angles. Prove: $\angle 1 \cong \angle 3$.

StatementReason
1. $\angle 1$ and $\angle 2$ form a linear pairDefinition of linear pair
2. $m\angle 1 + m\angle 2 = 180°$Linear Pair Postulate
3. $\angle 2$ and $\angle 3$ form a linear pairDefinition of linear pair
4. $m\angle 2 + m\angle 3 = 180°$Linear Pair Postulate
5. $m\angle 1 + m\angle 2 = m\angle 2 + m\angle 3$Substitution (steps 2 and 4)
6. $m\angle 1 = m\angle 3$Subtraction Property of Equality
7. $\angle 1 \cong \angle 3$Definition of congruent angles

2.6 Proving Angle Relationships

Vertical Angles Theorem

If two lines intersect, then the vertical angles formed are congruent.

(The proof is shown in Example 2.5b above.)

Congruent Supplements Theorem

If two angles are supplementary to the same angle (or to congruent angles), then the two angles are congruent.

Formally: if $\angle 1 \cong \angle 2$ and $\angle 1$ and $\angle 3$ are supplementary and $\angle 2$ and $\angle 4$ are supplementary, then $\angle 3 \cong \angle 4$.

Congruent Complements Theorem

If two angles are complementary to the same angle (or to congruent angles), then the two angles are congruent.

Linear Pair Postulate

If two angles form a linear pair, then they are supplementary: $m\angle 1 + m\angle 2 = 180°$.

Practice Problems

Unit 2 Practice — 10 Problems

  1. Find the next three terms and state the conjecture: $3, 6, 12, 24, \ldots$
  2. Give a counterexample to disprove: "The sum of two prime numbers is always odd."
  3. Write the converse, inverse, and contrapositive of: "If two angles are vertical angles, then they are congruent."
  4. Is the converse in Problem 3 true or false? Explain.
  5. Use the Law of Detachment: "If a polygon is a triangle, then it has exactly three sides." Alex draws a polygon with three sides. What can you conclude?
  6. Use the Law of Syllogism: (1) If you study hard, then you pass the test. (2) If you pass the test, then you earn a good grade. What can you conclude?
  7. State the property that justifies each step when solving $3(x - 2) = 15$:
    (a) $3x - 6 = 15$   (b) $3x = 21$   (c) $x = 7$.
  8. Write a two-column proof: Given $5x - 3 = 2x + 12$, prove $x = 5$.
  9. Two angles are supplementary and congruent. Find the measure of each angle. Justify each step.
  10. $\angle A$ and $\angle B$ are complementary. $\angle C$ and $\angle B$ are complementary. Using the Congruent Complements Theorem, what can you conclude about $\angle A$ and $\angle C$?
