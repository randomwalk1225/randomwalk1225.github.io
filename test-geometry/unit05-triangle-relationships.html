Unit 5 — Relationships Within Triangles

Grade 9–10 · Geometry · Topics: Midsegments, Triangle Centers, Triangle Inequality

5.1 Midsegments of a Triangle

Definition — Midsegment

A midsegment of a triangle connects the midpoints of two sides of the triangle. Every triangle has exactly three midsegments.

Theorem — Triangle Midsegment Theorem

A midsegment is parallel to the third side and exactly half its length.

If $D$ is the midpoint of $\overline{AB}$ and $E$ is the midpoint of $\overline{AC}$, then:

$$\overline{DE} \parallel \overline{BC} \quad \text{and} \quad DE = \tfrac{1}{2}\,BC$$

Example 5.1

In $\triangle ABC$, $\overline{DE}$ is a midsegment parallel to $\overline{BC}$, where $D$ is the midpoint of $\overline{AB}$ and $E$ is the midpoint of $\overline{AC}$. If $BC = 14$, find $DE$.

Solution: By the Midsegment Theorem, $DE = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot BC = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 14 = \boxed{7}$.

5.2 Perpendicular Bisectors and the Circumcenter

Definition — Perpendicular Bisector

The perpendicular bisector of a segment is a line that is perpendicular to the segment and passes through its midpoint.

Theorem — Perpendicular Bisector Theorem

A point lies on the perpendicular bisector of a segment if and only if it is equidistant from the two endpoints of the segment.

If $P$ is on the ⊥ bisector of $\overline{AB}$, then $PA = PB$.

Definition — Circumcenter

The circumcenter is the point where the three perpendicular bisectors of a triangle meet. It is the center of the circumscribed circle (the circle passing through all three vertices).

The circumcenter is equidistant from all three vertices: $CA = CB = CC'$ (circumradius).

Tip — Location of the Circumcenter

  • Acute triangle: Circumcenter is inside the triangle.
  • Right triangle: Circumcenter lies on the hypotenuse (at its midpoint).
  • Obtuse triangle: Circumcenter is outside the triangle.

5.3 Angle Bisectors and the Incenter

Theorem — Angle Bisector Theorem

A point lies on the bisector of an angle if and only if it is equidistant from the two sides of the angle (measured perpendicularly).

Definition — Incenter

The incenter is the point where the three angle bisectors of a triangle meet. It is the center of the inscribed circle (the largest circle that fits inside the triangle).

The incenter is always inside the triangle, regardless of triangle type.

5.4 Medians and the Centroid

Definition — Median

A median of a triangle connects a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. Every triangle has three medians.

Theorem — Centroid Theorem

The three medians of a triangle meet at the centroid, which divides each median in a ratio of 2 : 1 from the vertex to the opposite side.

$$\frac{\text{vertex to centroid}}{\text{centroid to midpoint}} = \frac{2}{1}$$

Equivalently: the centroid is $\frac{2}{3}$ of the way along each median from the vertex.

Example 5.4

Centroid $G$ lies on median $\overline{AM}$. If $AG = 16$, find $GM$.

Solution: By the Centroid Theorem, $AG : GM = 2 : 1$, so:

$$GM = \frac{AG}{2} = \frac{16}{2} = \boxed{8}$$

5.5 Altitudes and the Orthocenter

Definition — Altitude

An altitude of a triangle is a perpendicular segment from a vertex to the line containing the opposite side (or its extension). It represents the "height" of the triangle from that vertex.

Definition — Orthocenter

The orthocenter is the point where the three altitudes (or their extensions) meet.

  • Acute triangle: Orthocenter is inside the triangle.
  • Right triangle: Orthocenter is at the vertex of the right angle.
  • Obtuse triangle: Orthocenter is outside the triangle.

5.6 Triangle Inequality Theorem

Theorem — Triangle Inequality

The sum of the lengths of any two sides of a triangle must be greater than the length of the third side.

$$a + b > c, \quad a + c > b, \quad b + c > a$$

Example 5.6a — Can Three Lengths Form a Triangle?

Can sides of length 5, 7, and 13 form a triangle?

Check the critical inequality (smallest two vs. largest):

$$5 + 7 = 12 < 13$$

Since $12 < 13$, the condition fails. No, these lengths cannot form a triangle.

Example 5.6b — Range for the Third Side

Two sides of a triangle have lengths 4 and 9. Find all possible values for the third side $x$.

Solution: The third side must satisfy both:

$$x + 4 > 9 \implies x > 5$$ $$4 + 9 > x \implies x < 13$$

Therefore: $\boxed{5 < x < 13}$.

Quick formula: $|b - a| < x < a + b$, so $|9 - 4| < x < 9 + 4 \implies 5 < x < 13$.

5.7 Inequalities in Triangles

Theorem — Side-Angle Inequality

In any triangle:

  • The longest side is opposite the largest angle.
  • The shortest side is opposite the smallest angle.
  • Equal sides are opposite equal angles (isosceles triangle property).

In $\triangle ABC$: if $\angle A > \angle B > \angle C$, then $BC > AC > AB$.

Practice Problems

Unit 5 Practice — 10 Problems

  1. In $\triangle PQR$, midsegment $\overline{ST}$ is parallel to $\overline{QR}$. If $QR = 22$, find $ST$.
  2. Midsegment $\overline{MN}$ of $\triangle ABC$ has length 9. Find $BC$.
  3. Point $P$ is on the perpendicular bisector of $\overline{XY}$ where $XY = 10$. If $PX = 13$, find $PY$.
  4. The circumcenter of a triangle is located outside the triangle. What type of triangle is it?
  5. Centroid $G$ of $\triangle ABC$ lies on median $\overline{BM}$. If $GM = 5$, find $BG$ and $BM$.
  6. Centroid $G$ divides median $\overline{CF}$. If $CF = 24$, find $CG$ and $GF$.
  7. Can a triangle have sides of length 3, 8, and 12? Explain.
  8. Can a triangle have sides of length 6, 9, and 14? Explain.
  9. Two sides of a triangle are 7 and 11. Write an inequality for the third side $x$.
  10. In $\triangle DEF$, $\angle D = 80°$, $\angle E = 60°$, $\angle F = 40°$. List the sides from shortest to longest.
