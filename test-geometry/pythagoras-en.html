The Pythagorean Theorem

Geometry • Middle & High School • MathHub Global • February 2026

The Pythagorean Theorem is one of the most famous results in all of mathematics. It connects the three sides of any right triangle with a single elegant equation, and it appears everywhere ‚Äî from architecture and navigation to computer graphics and physics.

The Theorem

Pythagorean Theorem

In a right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the two legs:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

where $c$ is the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) and $a$, $b$ are the two legs.

Key Vocabulary

  • Hypotenuse: the longest side, always opposite the right angle (90¬∞)
  • Legs: the two shorter sides that form the right angle
  • Right angle: an angle of exactly 90¬∞, marked with a small square

Proofs of the Pythagorean Theorem

There are over 370 known proofs. Here are five of the most elegant.

Proof 1 ‚Äî Rearrangement (Visual)

Take a square of side $(a+b)$. Fill it with four congruent right triangles (legs $a$, $b$, hypotenuse $c$). The leftover area in the centre is a square of side $c$, so: $$(a+b)^2 = 4 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}ab + c^2 \implies a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \qquad \square$$

Proof 2 ‚Äî Two-Stage Rearrangement

Start with the same $(a+b)^2$ outer square and four congruent right triangles. Arrange them two different ways to expose different inner areas ‚Äî both equal $(a+b)^2$:

  • Arrangement 1: 4 triangles leave a tilted $c^2$ square in the centre.
  • Arrangement 2: 4 triangles leave an $a^2$ square and a $b^2$ square.

Since both inner areas equal $(a+b)^2 - 4\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}ab$, we get $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$. $\square$

⇒

Proof 3 ‚Äî Garfield's Trapezoid (1876)

US President James Garfield discovered this proof in 1876. Arrange two congruent right triangles and one isosceles right triangle to form a trapezoid.

Algebra: Trapezoid area $= \frac{1}{2}(a+b)(a+b) = \frac{1}{2}(a^2+2ab+b^2)$. The three triangles give $\frac{1}{2}ab + \frac{1}{2}ab + \frac{1}{2}c^2$. Setting equal and simplifying: $\boldsymbol{a^2 + b^2 = c^2}$.

Proof 4 ‚Äî Similar Triangles (Altitude on Hypotenuse)

Drop an altitude from the right-angle vertex $C$ to the hypotenuse $AB$, meeting it at $H$. This creates three similar triangles.

From $\triangle ACH \sim \triangle ABC$: $\dfrac{p}{a} = \dfrac{a}{c}$ so $a^2 = pc$.
From $\triangle CBH \sim \triangle ABC$: $\dfrac{q}{b} = \dfrac{b}{c}$ so $b^2 = qc$.
Adding: $a^2 + b^2 = pc + qc = c(p+q) = c \cdot c = \boldsymbol{c^2} \quad\square$

Proof 5 ‚Äî Euclid‚Äôs Proof (Elements Book I, Prop. 47)

Draw squares on all three sides, then drop altitude $CL$ from the right-angle vertex $C$ to hypotenuse $AB$, extended to $M$ on the far side of $c^2$. Line $CM$ splits the large square into two rectangles ‚Äî each equal to one leg-square.

Euclid‚Äôs Key Steps

  1. Draw squares $b^2$, $a^2$, $c^2$ on sides $AC$, $BC$, $AB$. Drop altitude $CL$ to $AB$; extend to $M$ on $FG$.
  2. $\triangle ABE \cong \triangle AFC$ (SAS): $AB{=}AF{=}c$, $AE{=}AC{=}b$, $\angle BAE{=}\angle CAF$.
  3. $AF \parallel CL$ (both $\perp AB$), so $\triangle AFC = \triangle AFL$ (same base $AF$, equal height).
  4. $\square ACDE = 2\,\triangle ACE = 2\,\triangle ABE = 2\,\triangle AFC = 2\,\triangle AFL = \square AFML = b^2$.
  5. Similarly $\square CBHI = \square LBGM = a^2$. Therefore $\boldsymbol{c^2 = b^2 + a^2}$. $\square$

Finding the Hypotenuse

When you know both legs, substitute into $a^2+b^2=c^2$ and take the square root.

Example 1 ‚Äî Classic 3-4-5 Triangle

A right triangle has legs $a=3$ and $b=4$. Find the hypotenuse $c$.

Solution:

$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25$$ $$c = \sqrt{25} = \boxed{5}$$

Example 2 ‚Äî Non-Integer Answer

A right triangle has legs $a=5$ and $b=7$. Find $c$ (to 2 decimal places).

Solution:

$$c^2 = 5^2 + 7^2 = 25 + 49 = 74$$ $$c = \sqrt{74} \approx \boxed{8.60}$$

Finding a Missing Leg

When you know the hypotenuse and one leg, rearrange: $a^2 = c^2 - b^2$.

Example 3 ‚Äî Missing Leg

A right triangle has hypotenuse $c=13$ and leg $b=5$. Find leg $a$.

Solution:

$$a^2 = c^2 - b^2 = 13^2 - 5^2 = 169 - 25 = 144$$ $$a = \sqrt{144} = \boxed{12}$$

Tip ‚Äî Generating Triples

Any multiple of a Pythagorean triple is also a triple. If $(3,4,5)$ works, so does $(6,8,10)$, $(15,20,25)$, etc. You can also generate new primitives using $m^2-n^2,\;2mn,\;m^2+n^2$ for integers $m>n>0$.

The Converse

Converse of the Pythagorean Theorem

If $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ (where $c$ is the longest side), then the triangle is a right triangle.

If $a^2 + b^2 > c^2$ ‚Üí acute triangle  |  If $a^2 + b^2 < c^2$ ‚Üí obtuse triangle

Example 4 ‚Äî Is It a Right Triangle?

Determine whether each set of side lengths forms a right triangle.

(a) $6, 8, 10$

$$6^2 + 8^2 = 36 + 64 = 100 = 10^2 \quad \checkmark \text{ Right triangle}$$

(b) $5, 7, 9$

$$5^2 + 7^2 = 25 + 49 = 74 \neq 81 = 9^2 \quad \text{Not a right triangle}$$

Since $74 < 81$, we have $a^2+b^2 < c^2$ ‚Üí obtuse triangle.

Real-World Applications

Example 5 ‚Äî Diagonal of a Rectangle

A rectangle is 8 m wide and 6 m tall. How long is its diagonal?

$$d = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{64 + 36} = \sqrt{100} = \boxed{10 \text{ m}}$$

Example 6 ‚Äî Ladder Against a Wall

A 10-ft ladder leans against a vertical wall. The foot of the ladder is 6 ft from the base of the wall. How high up the wall does the ladder reach?

$$h^2 = 10^2 - 6^2 = 100 - 36 = 64 \implies h = \boxed{8 \text{ ft}}$$

Example 7 ‚Äî Distance Between Two Points

Find the distance between $P(1, 2)$ and $Q(7, 10)$.

$$d = \sqrt{(7-1)^2 + (10-2)^2} = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100} = \boxed{10}$$

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting which side is $c$: $c$ is always the hypotenuse ‚Äî the longest side.
  • Wrong rearrangement: To find a leg, use $a^2 = c^2 - b^2$, NOT $c^2 + b^2$.
  • Skipping the square root: $c^2 = 25$ means $c = 5$, not $c = 25$.
  • Applying to non-right triangles: The theorem only works for right triangles.
