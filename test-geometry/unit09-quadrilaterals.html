Unit 9 — Quadrilaterals and Polygons

Grade 9–10 · Geometry · MathHub

9.1 Polygons

Definition — Polygon

A polygon is a closed plane figure formed by three or more line segments (sides) that meet only at their endpoints (vertices).

  • Convex polygon: All interior angles are less than 180°. Any line segment connecting two interior points lies entirely inside the polygon.
  • Concave polygon: At least one interior angle is greater than 180° (a "cave" pushes inward).
  • Regular polygon: All sides are congruent AND all angles are congruent (equilateral and equiangular).

Theorem — Polygon Angle Sums

Interior Angle Sum of a convex polygon with $n$ sides:

$$S = (n-2) \times 180°$$

Exterior Angle Sum of any convex polygon = always $360°$, regardless of the number of sides.

For a regular $n$-gon, each interior angle $= \dfrac{(n-2)\times 180°}{n}$ and each exterior angle $= \dfrac{360°}{n}$.

Example — Hexagon Angles

Find the interior angle sum of a hexagon ($n = 6$).

$S = (6 - 2) \times 180° = 4 \times 180° = \mathbf{720°}$

Each interior angle of a regular hexagon:

$\dfrac{720°}{6} = \mathbf{120°}$

9.2 Parallelograms

Definition — Parallelogram

A parallelogram is a quadrilateral with both pairs of opposite sides parallel. Notation: $ABCD$ is a parallelogram written $\square ABCD$.

Theorem — Properties of Parallelograms

  • Opposite sides are congruent: $AB \cong CD$ and $BC \cong DA$.
  • Opposite angles are congruent: $\angle A \cong \angle C$ and $\angle B \cong \angle D$.
  • Consecutive angles are supplementary: $\angle A + \angle B = 180°$.
  • Diagonals bisect each other: The diagonals intersect at their midpoints.
Parallelogram ABCD

Example — Finding Side Lengths

$ABCD$ is a parallelogram. $AB = 2x + 3$ and $CD = x + 7$. Find $AB$.

Since opposite sides are congruent: $AB = CD$

$2x + 3 = x + 7$

$x = 4$

$AB = 2(4) + 3 = \mathbf{11}$

9.3 Proving Quadrilaterals Are Parallelograms

5 Ways to Prove a Quadrilateral is a Parallelogram

  1. Both pairs of opposite sides are parallel (definition).
  2. Both pairs of opposite sides are congruent.
  3. One pair of opposite sides is both parallel and congruent.
  4. The diagonals bisect each other.
  5. Both pairs of opposite angles are congruent.

Any one of these five conditions is sufficient to conclude the quadrilateral is a parallelogram.

9.4 Rectangles

Theorem — Rectangle

A rectangle is a parallelogram with four right angles. It inherits all parallelogram properties, plus:

  • All four interior angles are $90°$.
  • Diagonals are congruent: $AC \cong BD$.

Example — Rectangle Diagonals

Rectangle $ABCD$ has diagonal $AC = 3x - 1$ and diagonal $BD = x + 9$. Find $x$.

Since diagonals of a rectangle are congruent: $AC = BD$

$3x - 1 = x + 9$

$2x = 10$

$x = \mathbf{5}$

Check: $AC = 3(5)-1 = 14$, $BD = 5+9 = 14$ ✓

9.5 Rhombuses and Squares

Theorem — Rhombus

A rhombus is a parallelogram with all four sides congruent. Additional properties:

  • All sides are congruent: $AB = BC = CD = DA$.
  • Diagonals are perpendicular: $AC \perp BD$.
  • Diagonals bisect the opposite angles: each diagonal bisects the pair of vertex angles it connects.

Definition — Square

A square is both a rectangle and a rhombus — it has four right angles AND all four sides are congruent. A square is the most specific type of parallelogram, inheriting all properties of parallelograms, rectangles, and rhombuses.

9.6 Trapezoids

Definition — Trapezoid

A trapezoid is a quadrilateral with exactly one pair of parallel sides. The parallel sides are called bases ($b_1$ and $b_2$), and the non-parallel sides are called legs.

Theorem — Midsegment of a Trapezoid

The midsegment (or median) of a trapezoid connects the midpoints of the two legs. It is:

  • Parallel to both bases.
  • Equal in length to half the sum of the bases: $m = \dfrac{b_1 + b_2}{2}$

Theorem — Isosceles Trapezoid

An isosceles trapezoid has congruent legs. Additional properties:

  • The two base angles (on either base) are congruent.
  • The diagonals are congruent: $AC \cong BD$.

Example — Trapezoid Midsegment

A trapezoid has midsegment $= 11$ and one base $= 8$. Find the other base.

Using the midsegment formula: $11 = \dfrac{8 + x}{2}$

$22 = 8 + x$

$x = \mathbf{14}$

9.7 Kites

Theorem — Kite

A kite is a quadrilateral with two pairs of consecutive congruent sides (adjacent sides, not opposite). Properties:

  • Two pairs of consecutive sides are congruent.
  • Diagonals are perpendicular: $d_1 \perp d_2$.
  • Exactly one pair of opposite angles is congruent (the angles between the unequal sides).
  • One diagonal bisects the other.

Practice Problems

Unit 9 Practice — 12 Problems

  1. Find the interior angle sum of an octagon ($n = 8$).
  2. Find each interior angle of a regular pentagon.
  3. How many sides does a polygon have if its interior angle sum is $1440°$?
  4. $PQRS$ is a parallelogram. $\angle P = 3x + 10°$ and $\angle R = x + 50°$. Find $\angle P$.
  5. In $\square ABCD$, $AB = 4y - 1$ and $CD = 2y + 7$. Find $AB$.
  6. The diagonals of a parallelogram intersect at $M$. If $AM = 3x - 2$ and $CM = x + 6$, find $AC$.
  7. A quadrilateral has diagonals that bisect each other. What type of quadrilateral must it be?
  8. Rectangle $WXYZ$ has diagonal $WY = 4x + 3$ and diagonal $XZ = 6x - 9$. Find $WY$.
  9. A rhombus has one diagonal of length 24 and another of length 10. Find the side length of the rhombus.
  10. A trapezoid has bases of length 6 and 14. Find the length of its midsegment.
  11. An isosceles trapezoid has base angles of $65°$ each. Find the other two angles.
  12. Classify the quadrilateral with four congruent sides and four right angles. List all the special quadrilateral categories it belongs to.
