Unit 1 — Foundations of Geometry

Unit 1 of 13 · Grade 9–10 · Common Core G-CO.1

Geometry begins with a handful of fundamental ideas that cannot be formally defined — yet everything else in the subject is built upon them. In this unit you will meet the basic building blocks of geometry: points, lines, and planes. From there you will learn how to measure segments and angles precisely, identify special angle pairs, and perform classical compass-and-straightedge constructions. Mastering these foundations makes every later topic — from triangle congruence to coordinate proof — far easier to understand.

1.1 Points, Lines, and Planes

Undefined Terms

Geometry starts with three terms that are accepted without formal definition:

  • Point — a location in space with no size or dimension. Named with a capital letter, e.g. point $A$.
  • Line — a straight, one-dimensional figure extending infinitely in both directions. Named by two points on it ($\overleftrightarrow{AB}$) or a lowercase letter.
  • Plane — a flat, two-dimensional surface extending infinitely in all directions. Named by three non-collinear points or a script letter (plane $\mathcal{P}$).

Collinear points lie on the same line. Coplanar points lie in the same plane. If three or more points are not all on one line they are non-collinear.

Figure 1.1 — Points, Line, and Plane

Key notation: the line through $A$ and $B$ is written $\overleftrightarrow{AB}$; the ray starting at $A$ through $B$ is $\overrightarrow{AB}$; the segment from $A$ to $B$ is $\overline{AB}$.

1.2 Measuring Segments

Segments and Length

Segment $\overline{AB}$ consists of endpoints $A$ and $B$ and all points between them. Its length (distance) is written $AB$ or $|AB|$ — a non-negative real number.

Two segments are congruent ($\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}$) when they have equal length ($AB = CD$).

Ruler Postulate

The points on a line can be matched one-to-one with the real numbers. The distance between two points equals the absolute value of the difference of their coordinates:

$$AB = |x_B - x_A|$$

Segment Addition Postulate

If $B$ is between $A$ and $C$, then

$$AB + BC = AC$$

Example 1.2 — Segment Addition

Given: $B$ is between $A$ and $C$. $AB = 5$, $AC = 12$. Find $BC$.

Solution:

$$BC = AC - AB = 12 - 5 = 7$$

The midpoint $M$ of $\overline{AB}$ is the point exactly halfway between $A$ and $B$. In the coordinate plane, if $A = (x_1, y_1)$ and $B = (x_2, y_2)$:

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\; \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

1.3 Measuring Angles

Angles

An angle is formed by two rays (sides) sharing a common endpoint (vertex). Angle $ABC$ is written $\angle ABC$ — the vertex is always the middle letter.

  • Acute: $0° < m\angle < 90°$
  • Right: $m\angle = 90°$
  • Obtuse: $90° < m\angle < 180°$
  • Straight: $m\angle = 180°$
Figure 1.3 — Angle Types

Angle Addition Postulate

If point $D$ is in the interior of $\angle ABC$, then

$$m\angle ABD + m\angle DBC = m\angle ABC$$

1.4 Angle Pairs

Special Angle Pairs

  • Complementary angles: two angles whose measures sum to $90°$.
  • Supplementary angles: two angles whose measures sum to $180°$.
  • Linear pair: two adjacent supplementary angles whose non-common sides form a straight line.
  • Vertical angles: two non-adjacent angles formed by two intersecting lines. They share only a vertex.

Vertical Angles Theorem

Vertical angles are congruent.

If two lines intersect and form $\angle 1$ and $\angle 3$ as vertical angles, then $\angle 1 \cong \angle 3$, i.e. $m\angle 1 = m\angle 3$.

Example 1.4 — Supplementary Angle Equation

Given: $\angle 1$ and $\angle 2$ are supplementary. $m\angle 1 = (3x + 10)°$, $m\angle 2 = (x + 30)°$. Find $x$.

Solution:

$$m\angle 1 + m\angle 2 = 180°$$ $$(3x + 10) + (x + 30) = 180$$ $$4x + 40 = 180$$ $$4x = 140$$ $$x = 35°$$

1.5 Basic Constructions

Six Fundamental Compass-and-Straightedge Constructions

  1. Copy a segment: set compass to length $AB$, mark off the same length from a new point.
  2. Copy an angle: transfer the arc and chord of the original angle to the new location.
  3. Bisect a segment (perpendicular bisector): draw arcs from both endpoints; the line through the intersections is the perpendicular bisector and passes through the midpoint.
  4. Bisect an angle: draw an arc from the vertex, then arcs from where that arc crosses the sides; connect the vertex to their intersection.
  5. Construct a perpendicular line through a point: draw arcs from the point to intersect the line at two places; bisect that segment.
  6. Construct a parallel line through a point: copy the angle that the original line makes with a transversal; the new ray is parallel to the original line.

Practice Problems

Unit 1 Practice — 10 Problems

  1. $M$ is the midpoint of $\overline{AB}$. $AM = 3x - 4$ and $MB = x + 8$. Find $AM$, $MB$, and $AB$.
  2. $Q$ is between $P$ and $R$. $PQ = 2x + 1$, $QR = x + 3$, $PR = 25$. Find $x$ and $QR$.
  3. Find the midpoint of the segment with endpoints $(-3, 7)$ and $(5, -1)$.
  4. Classify each angle as acute, right, obtuse, or straight: (a) $47°$ (b) $90°$ (c) $133°$ (d) $180°$.
  5. $\angle PQS$ and $\angle SQR$ are adjacent with $m\angle PQR = 142°$. If $m\angle PQS = 5x + 2$ and $m\angle SQR = 3x + 8$, find $x$.
  6. Using the Angle Addition Postulate, find $m\angle WXZ$ if $m\angle WXY = 55°$ and $m\angle YXZ = 30°$.
  7. $\angle A$ and $\angle B$ are complementary. $m\angle A = 2x + 10$ and $m\angle B = 3x$. Find both angles.
  8. Two angles form a linear pair. One angle measures $(7x - 3)°$ and the other $(4x + 15)°$. Find both measures.
  9. Two lines intersect. One angle formed measures $68°$. Find the measures of the other three angles.
  10. $\angle 1$ and $\angle 2$ are vertical angles. $m\angle 1 = 5x + 12$ and $m\angle 2 = 8x - 9$. Find $m\angle 1$.
