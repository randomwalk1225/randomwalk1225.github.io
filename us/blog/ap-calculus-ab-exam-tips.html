MCQ Strategy

The MCQ section is worth 50% of your score. Here's how to maximize it:

Part A (No Calculator — 30 questions, 60 min)

  • Average 2 minutes per question. If you spend more than 2.5 minutes on any single problem, skip and come back.
  • Know your derivatives cold. Chain rule, product rule, quotient rule — these must be automatic. Any hesitation here costs time on harder problems.
  • Elimination works well. On limit questions, plug in values or factor to eliminate 2–3 wrong answers quickly.
  • No penalty for wrong answers — always guess if you skip. Never leave a blank.

Part B (Calculator — 15 questions, 45 min)

  • Use the calculator strategically. The calculator is for numerical integration, derivative at a point, and graphical analysis — not for basic algebra.
  • Store intersections and values. When finding areas between curves, store the intersection x-values before computing integrals.
  • Verify with graph. If an answer looks off, graph the function quickly to sanity-check your setup.

FRQ Tactics

The 6 free-response questions test your ability to communicate mathematical reasoning clearly. Scoring is rubric-based — you earn points for specific steps, not just final answers.

Golden rule: Show all your work, even if you use a calculator for Part A. A correct final answer with no supporting work earns 0 points. An incorrect answer with correct setup earns partial credit.

The 4 Most Common FRQ Types

  1. Rate/Accumulation problems — always set up the integral with correct limits and units. Write the integral expression before evaluating it numerically.
  2. Particle motion — position, velocity, acceleration. Know when a particle changes direction (v(t) = 0 and sign change). Know total vs. net distance.
  3. Table/graph-based questions — the function is given as data or a graph, not an equation. You must use the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to relate integrals and derivatives.
  4. Differential equations — separation of variables, general vs. particular solution, slope fields. Always include the constant of integration "+C" in your general solution.

Communication Tips

  • Write integral and derivative notation correctly. Missing dx in an integral costs a point.
  • State your reasoning for each step (e.g., "Since f'(x) changes from negative to positive at x=2, f has a local minimum there").
  • Justify absolute extrema by checking all critical points AND endpoints.
  • Include units on final answers when the problem involves a real-world context.

Common Mistakes

Top 5 mistakes that cost students a 5:
  1. Forgetting the "+C" on indefinite integrals (automatic point deduction)
  2. Not checking endpoints when finding absolute extrema on a closed interval
  3. Confusing the derivative of ln|x| with 1/x (sign errors with absolute values)
  4. Setting up wrong limits of integration (using y-values instead of x-values, or swapping bounds)
  5. Not distinguishing between net displacement and total distance traveled
