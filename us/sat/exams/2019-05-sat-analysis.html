May 2019 SAT Math Research Analysis

A detailed investigation into polynomial manipulation, rational functions, and advanced algebraic structure trends in the May 2019 administration.

Executive Summary

The May 2019 SAT administration highlighted the importance of algebraic fluency and the manipulation of complex rational expressions. Section 3 featured multi-step algebraic manipulations that rewarded efficient factoring and a deep understanding of function notation.

2019 global data shows an average Math score of 528, with the May sitting emphasizing core geometry principles alongside Passport to Advanced Math topics.

01. Conceptual Shifts: Complex Rational Equations

The May 2019 exam showed a clear preference for questions that required students to manipulate rational expressions. Instead of simple addition, students were often asked to identify which expression is equivalent to a complex fraction. This tests the ability to find common denominators and simplify polynomial numerators efficiently.

There was also a notable emphasis on Function Notation, where students had to interpret \( f(g(x)) \) or find \( x \) given a specific value of \( f(x) \) in a graphical context.

02. Topic Distribution: Radicals and Circle Geometry

We saw a return to core geometry principles, specifically Arc Length and Sector Area calculations. Additionally, radical equations appeared in the no-calculator section, requiring students to check for extraneous solutions.

  • Algebraic Fluency: The Polynomial Remainder Theorem was tested conceptually (identifying factors from remainders).
  • Geometry: Tangent lines to circles appeared, requiring knowledge that a tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency.
  • Complex Numbers: Addition and subtraction of complex numbers provided some of the easier points in Section 4.

03. Strategic Recommendations

For future test-takers, we recommend a heavy focus on polynomial division and factoring. The May 2019 sitting proved that the SAT is moving away from simple quadratic factoring into higher-degree polynomial transformations.

Understanding the relationship between roots, factors, and remainders is now a prerequisite for a 750+ score.
