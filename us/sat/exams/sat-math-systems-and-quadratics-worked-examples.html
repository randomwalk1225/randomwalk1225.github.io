Digital SAT Math: 4 Worked Examples on Systems, Quadratics & Growth

Two of the highest-value question types on the Digital SAT Math section are systems of equations (Heart of Algebra) and quadratics (Passport to Advanced Math). Below are four problems written in the SAT's style and solved completely — the fast method first, then the trap that most often costs the point.

Written and solved by Daniel Park, math educator (MathHub). Last updated June 12, 2026. Every problem is original and the answers are verifiable by hand.

The Digital SAT rewards a clean method over heavy computation. For each problem we show the reasoning the way a strong test-taker actually thinks, not just the final number. Work each one yourself before reading the solution.

Example 1 · Systems of equationsA linear system in context

A coffee shop sells small drinks for \(\$3\) and large drinks for \(\$5\). On one day it sold a total of \(80\) drinks and collected \(\$310\). How many large drinks did it sell?

1Name the variables and write two equations. Let \(s\) = small drinks, \(\ell\) = large drinks.
\[ s + \ell = 80, \qquad 3s + 5\ell = 310. \]
2Substitute \(s = 80 - \ell\) into the money equation:
\[ 3(80 - \ell) + 5\ell = 310 \;\Longrightarrow\; 240 - 3\ell + 5\ell = 310. \]
3Solve:
\[ 240 + 2\ell = 310 \;\Longrightarrow\; 2\ell = 70 \;\Longrightarrow\; \ell = 35. \]
35 large drinks. Check: \(s = 45\), and \(3(45) + 5(35) = 135 + 175 = 310\). ✓
Trap
The question asks for large drinks. If you solve for \(s\) first you get \(45\) — a number that appears in the answer choices specifically to catch students who answer the wrong variable. Underline what is asked before you start.

Example 2 · QuadraticsOne real solution (discriminant)

In the equation \(x^2 - 6x + c = 0\), \(c\) is a constant. If the equation has exactly one real solution, what is the value of \(c\)?

1"Exactly one real solution" means the discriminant is zero. For \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\), the discriminant is \(b^2 - 4ac\).
2Here \(a = 1\), \(b = -6\), constant term \(= c\). Set the discriminant to zero:
\[ (-6)^2 - 4(1)(c) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; 36 - 4c = 0. \]
3Solve:
\[ 4c = 36 \;\Longrightarrow\; c = 9. \]
\(c = 9\). Then \(x^2 - 6x + 9 = (x-3)^2 = 0\), a single (double) root at \(x = 3\). ✓
Trap
Don't confuse the cases: discriminant \(> 0\) gives two real solutions, \(= 0\) gives exactly one, \(< 0\) gives none. "Exactly one real solution" is always the \(= 0\) case.

Example 3 · Passport to Advanced MathWhere a line meets a parabola

The graphs of \(y = x^2 - 3x + 2\) and \(y = 2x - 2\) intersect at two points. What is the sum of the \(x\)-coordinates of those points?

1Set the expressions equal (the points share the same \(y\)):
\[ x^2 - 3x + 2 = 2x - 2. \]
2Move everything to one side:
\[ x^2 - 3x + 2 - 2x + 2 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x^2 - 5x + 4 = 0. \]
3Factor and read off the roots:
\[ (x - 1)(x - 4) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x = 1 \text{ or } x = 4. \]
Sum of \(x\)-coordinates \(= 1 + 4 = 5\).
Faster method
You never needed to factor. For \(x^2 - 5x + 4 = 0\), the sum of the roots is \(-\tfrac{b}{a} = -\tfrac{-5}{1} = 5\) (Vieta's formulas). On the Digital SAT this turns a 60-second problem into a 10-second one.

Example 4 · Exponential growthDoubling

A bacteria culture starts with \(200\) cells and doubles every \(3\) hours. How many cells are present after \(12\) hours?

1Model exponential growth as \(N = N_0 \cdot 2^{(t/T)}\), where \(N_0 = 200\) is the start, \(T = 3\) is the doubling time, and \(t\) is elapsed time.
2Find how many doublings occur in \(12\) hours:
\[ \frac{t}{T} = \frac{12}{3} = 4. \]
3Apply four doublings:
\[ N = 200 \cdot 2^{4} = 200 \cdot 16 = 3200. \]
3200 cells.
Trap
Don't multiply \(200\) by \(2\) and then by \(12\). The growth is by a factor of \(2\) for each 3-hour period, so the exponent is the number of periods \(\bigl(\tfrac{12}{3}=4\bigr)\), not the number of hours.
