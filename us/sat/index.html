What These Reports Cover

The SAT Math section tests a focused set of algebraic, data analysis, and geometry concepts across 44 questions (27 calculator, 17 no-calculator in the paper format — or two adaptive modules in the Digital SAT). But not all questions are created equal: the distribution of topics, the difficulty clustering at specific question numbers, and the scoring curve vary significantly between administrations.

Each analysis report on this page breaks down an actual administered SAT into: topic-by-topic frequency counts, difficulty ratings derived from College Board's own data and student performance, score curve comparisons, and identification of the "killer problems" — the hard, multi-step questions at the end of each module that disproportionately affect scores above 700.

This data is useful whether you're a student targeting a specific score or a teacher designing a curriculum. Knowing that the March 2023 exam was unusually heavy on linear systems and inequalities, while October 2022 emphasized exponential growth and data interpretation, lets you allocate study time precisely.

SAT Math Structure at a Glance

The paper-format SAT (administered 2017–2023) divided its math section into two parts: a 25-minute no-calculator section (20 questions) and a 55-minute calculator section (38 questions). Questions are arranged roughly in order of difficulty, with the hardest items concentrated in positions 15–20 of each section.

The Digital SAT, which became the standard format for international students in 2023 and domestic US students in 2024, replaced this with two adaptive modules of 27 questions each, with the second module's difficulty calibrated to your first-module performance. Digital SAT practice tests 1–10 are analyzed in the second tab below.

Key Topic Areas Tested

College Board groups SAT math content into four domains. Based on our analysis across all released exams, here is how each domain typically appears:

  • Heart of Algebra (30–35%): Linear equations and inequalities, systems of equations, absolute value, and interpreting linear models. This is the largest domain and the area where most students can gain the most points through targeted practice.
  • Problem Solving & Data Analysis (25–30%): Ratios, proportions, percentages, unit conversions, scatterplots, two-way tables, and statistics (mean, median, standard deviation). Questions in this domain often use real-world contexts from science or social studies.
  • Passport to Advanced Math (25–30%): Polynomial expressions, factoring, quadratics, exponential functions, and function notation. The hardest questions in this domain require multi-step algebraic manipulation.
  • Additional Topics (5–10%): Geometry (area, volume, circles, triangles), trigonometry (basic SOHCAHTOA and special triangles), and complex numbers. These questions are fewer in number but can be high-difficulty.
