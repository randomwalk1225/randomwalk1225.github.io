Petals and Power Series: 2017 AP Calculus BC FRQ Analysis

Deep analysis of the 2017 BC exam, highlighting the complex polar intersection problems and the rigorous series tests in Question 6.

BC Summary

  • Difficulty: Moderate-Hard. Q2 (Polar) and Q6 (Series) were the primary points of differentiation.
  • Focus Area: High importance on Interval of Convergence and Polar Area between curves.
  • Calculator Strategy: Finding intersection angles for polar curves in Q2 was essential.

1. Conceptual Shifts: The Geometry of Polar Graphs

The 2017 BC exam pushed the boundaries of Polar Geometry (Unit 9). Question 2 didn't just ask for area; it asked for the area *inside* one curve and *outside* another. This requires a strong visual intuition of how \\( r=1+\sin\theta \\) and \\( r=2\cos\theta \\) behave on the coordinate plane. The shift is towards spatial reasoning within a calculus context.

In the Series section, the focus remained on the **Ratio Test**, but with an added layer of complexity in finding the interval of convergence and testing endpoints for conditional vs. absolute convergence.

2. Question Breakdown: The BC Core

  • Q2 (Polar Curves): Calculating the area shared by \\( r=1+\sin\theta \\) and \\( r=2\cos\theta \\). This required finding the intersection \\( \theta = \\\( \\frac{\\pi}{6} \\) \\) and setting up two separate integrals for different \\( \theta \\)-ranges.
  • Q5 (Improper Integrals): Evaluating \\( \int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x^2+px+q} \\( dx \\) \\) as a way to test partial fractions and limit behavior at infinity.
  • Q6 (Taylor Series): Defining a power series \\( \\( f(x) \\) = \sum (-1)^n \frac{2^n (x-1)^n}{n} \\). Students had to find the radius of convergence (1/2) and test the endpoints \\( (1/2, 3/2] \\).
  • Shared Questions: Q1 (Water Tank), Q3 (Graph), and Q4 (Internal Temp) were shared with AB, providing standard checks on accumulation and theorems.

3. Strategic Recommendations

For BC students, Polar Area Setup is often the deciding factor between a 4 and a 5. Practice sketching curves by hand so you can verify what your calculator shows. Furthermore, for Power Series, don't just find the radius; the interval test is where the easy points are found or lost. Always write out your Limit Comparison Test or Alternating Series Test justifications explicitly.
