02. Killer Problem Analysis

FRQ #4: Complex Area/Volume with rotation about a horizontal line

This question required students to find the volume of a solid generated by rotating a region bounded by two functions around a horizontal line (e.g., \( y = k \)). The primary hurdle was the setup of the "Washer Method" when the axis of rotation was located above or below the region.

Why Students Struggled:

Common pitfalls included reversing the order of functions in the outer vs. inner radius calculation, failing to find the correct intersection points for the limits of integration in the non-calculator section, and conceptual confusion between rotation about a horizontal line vs. cross-sections perpendicular to the \( y \)-axis.

03. Mark Scheme Insights

The 2025 scoring guidelines rewarded conceptual setups even when algebraic execution faltered. Key point distributions included:

  • Setup vs. Solve: In FRQ #4, 2 points were often awarded for the correct integrand (the squared radii setup) even if the final integration was incorrect.
  • Justification Rigor: 1 point was strictly reserved for the mention of "\( f'(x) \) changes sign" or "testing endpoints" for absolute extrema. Phrases like "the graph goes up then down" received 0 points.
  • Notation Precision: Deduction of 1 point for "linkage errors" (writing equal signs between unequal mathematical expressions) was more strictly enforced in 2025.
  • Units of Measure: Units were required for 1 point in FRQ #1 and FRQ #2; missing "feet/min" or "cubic inches" resulted in a lost point.

04. Strategic Advice for 2026-2027

Based on the 2025 data, students preparing for upcoming administrations should prioritize:

  • Geometric Visualization: Practice sketching regions and identifying radii for rotation about arbitrary lines (\( y = k \) or \( x = h \)). This is becoming a standard high-point-value topic.
  • Calculator Efficiency: For the calculator-active section, ensure you can store intersection points and use them as limits in numerical integration without rounding errors.
  • Formal Justification: Memorize the standard phrasing for the Mean Value Theorem (MVT) and the Candidates Test for absolute extrema.
