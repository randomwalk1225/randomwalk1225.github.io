02. Killer Problem Analysis

FRQ #6: Power Series & Lagrange Error Bound

As is tradition, the final FRQ (#6) focused on Infinite Series and was the most difficult question on the exam. Specifically, the inclusion of the Lagrange Error Bound proved to be a significant conceptual hurdle.

Why Students Struggled:

Many students failed to identify the correct value for \( M \) (the maximum value of the \( (n+1) \)-th derivative) or confused it with the Alternating Series Error Bound. Additionally, while most could perform the Ratio Test, many omitted the critical step of checking endpoints in the interval of convergence.

03. Mark Scheme Insights

Based on 2024 grading patterns, points were awarded for process even when the final answer was incorrect. Key "point-getters" included:

  • Using the Derivative: 1 point for correctly identifying and setting up the derivative or anti-derivative in the context of the problem.
  • Setup of Integral: 1-2 points for the correct limits of integration, even if the integrand had minor errors.
  • Final Answer & Units: 1-2 points for the final numeric value, but only if correct units (e.g., cubic feet per minute) were included as requested.
  • Chain Rule Application: In the parametric section, points were heavily weighted toward the correct application of \( dy/dx = (dy/dt) / (dx/dt) \).

04. Strategic Advice for 2025-2026

To excel in the upcoming exam cycles, students should focus on:

  • Mastering Series Error Bounds: Practice distinguishing between when to use Alternating Series Error vs. Lagrange Error.
  • Calculator Efficiency: In Section I Part B and FRQ #1-2, fluency with "Math-9" (NINT) and "Math-8" (NDERIV) is essential to save time for conceptual analysis.
  • Justification Language: Use specific phrasing for theorems (e.g., "Since \( f \) is continuous on \( [a,b] \) and differentiable on \( (a,b) \), by the MVT...") to ensure full credit.
