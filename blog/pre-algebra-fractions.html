Chapter 2: Fractions & Decimals

Pre-Algebra  •  Chapter 2 of 10  •  Updated February 2026

Fractions and decimals are two different ways of writing numbers that fall between the integers. A fraction expresses a part of a whole using a numerator and denominator; a decimal uses place value to the right of a decimal point. Mastering both representations — and the ability to convert between them — is essential for every area of mathematics from algebra to statistics.

2.1 Types of Fractions

Definition: Fraction

A fraction $\dfrac{a}{b}$ represents $a$ parts of a whole divided into $b$ equal parts, where $b \neq 0$. The number $a$ is the numerator and $b$ is the denominator.

Proper Fraction

Numerator < Denominator
Value between 0 and 1
Example: $\dfrac{3}{5}$

Improper Fraction

Numerator ≥ Denominator
Value ≥ 1
Example: $\dfrac{9}{4}$

Mixed Number

Integer + proper fraction
Value ≥ 1
Example: $2\dfrac{1}{4}$

Converting Between Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers

Improper → Mixed: Divide numerator by denominator. The quotient is the integer part; the remainder is the new numerator.

$$\frac{17}{5} = 3 \text{ R } 2 = 3\frac{2}{5}$$

Mixed → Improper: Multiply the integer by the denominator, then add the numerator.

$$4\frac{3}{7} = \frac{4 \times 7 + 3}{7} = \frac{31}{7}$$

2.2 Equivalent Fractions and Simplifying

Two fractions are equivalent if they represent the same value. We can create equivalent fractions by multiplying or dividing both numerator and denominator by the same nonzero number — this is the Fundamental Property of Fractions:

$$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \times k}{b \times k} \quad \text{for any } k \neq 0$$

A fraction is in simplest form (lowest terms) when the numerator and denominator share no common factor other than 1. To simplify, divide both by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF).

Example 2.1 — Simplifying a Fraction

Simplify $\dfrac{36}{48}$.

Step 1: Find GCF(36, 48).
Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36
Factors of 48: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 16, 24, 48
GCF = 12
Step 2: Divide both by 12.
$$\frac{36}{48} = \frac{36 \div 12}{48 \div 12} = \frac{3}{4}$$
Answer: $\dfrac{3}{4}$

2.3 LCM and GCF

Greatest Common Factor (GCF) & Least Common Multiple (LCM)

The GCF of two integers is the largest integer that divides both evenly. Used for simplifying fractions.

The LCM of two integers is the smallest positive integer that both divide evenly into. Used for finding common denominators.

Useful relationship: For positive integers $a$ and $b$: $$\text{GCF}(a,b) \times \text{LCM}(a,b) = a \times b$$

Prime Factorization Method

To find GCF and LCM of 12 and 18:

$12 = 2^2 \times 3$    $18 = 2 \times 3^2$

  • GCF: Take the lowest power of each common prime factor: $2^1 \times 3^1 = 6$
  • LCM: Take the highest power of each prime factor: $2^2 \times 3^2 = 36$

Check: $6 \times 36 = 216 = 12 \times 18$ ✓

2.4 Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Rules for Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Same denominator: Add or subtract the numerators; keep the denominator.

$$\frac{a}{c} + \frac{b}{c} = \frac{a+b}{c} \qquad \frac{a}{c} - \frac{b}{c} = \frac{a-b}{c}$$

Different denominators: Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD = LCM of denominators), rewrite each fraction with that denominator, then add or subtract.

Example 2.2 — Adding Fractions with Different Denominators

Compute $\dfrac{5}{6} + \dfrac{3}{8}$.

Step 1: Find LCD(6, 8). $6 = 2 \times 3$, $8 = 2^3$. LCD $= 2^3 \times 3 = 24$.
Step 2: Rewrite each fraction.
$$\frac{5}{6} = \frac{5 \times 4}{6 \times 4} = \frac{20}{24} \qquad \frac{3}{8} = \frac{3 \times 3}{8 \times 3} = \frac{9}{24}$$
Step 3: Add.
$$\frac{20}{24} + \frac{9}{24} = \frac{29}{24} = 1\frac{5}{24}$$
Answer: $1\dfrac{5}{24}$

Example 2.3 — Subtracting Mixed Numbers

Compute $5\dfrac{1}{3} - 2\dfrac{3}{4}$.

Method: Convert to improper fractions, then subtract.
$5\dfrac{1}{3} = \dfrac{16}{3}$    $2\dfrac{3}{4} = \dfrac{11}{4}$
LCD(3, 4) = 12.
$$\frac{16}{3} - \frac{11}{4} = \frac{64}{12} - \frac{33}{12} = \frac{31}{12} = 2\frac{7}{12}$$
Answer: $2\dfrac{7}{12}$

2.5 Multiplying Fractions

Multiplication Rule

Multiply numerators together and denominators together — no common denominator needed:

$$\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a \times c}{b \times d}$$

Always simplify before or after multiplying. Simplifying before (cross-canceling) keeps numbers smaller.

Example 2.4 — Multiplying Fractions with Cross-Canceling

Compute $\dfrac{9}{14} \times \dfrac{7}{12}$.

Cross-cancel: 9 and 12 share a factor of 3; 7 and 14 share a factor of 7.
$$\frac{\cancel{9}^{\,3}}{{\cancel{14}}^{\,2}} \times \frac{{\cancel{7}}^{\,1}}{{\cancel{12}}^{\,4}} = \frac{3}{2} \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{8}$$
Answer: $\dfrac{3}{8}$

Example 2.5 — Multiplying Mixed Numbers

Compute $3\dfrac{1}{2} \times 1\dfrac{3}{5}$.

Convert to improper: $\dfrac{7}{2} \times \dfrac{8}{5}$
Cross-cancel: 8 and 2 share factor 2.
$$\frac{7}{\cancel{2}} \times \frac{\cancel{8}^{\,4}}{5} = \frac{7 \times 4}{5} = \frac{28}{5} = 5\frac{3}{5}$$
Answer: $5\dfrac{3}{5}$

2.6 Dividing Fractions

Division Rule: Multiply by the Reciprocal

The reciprocal of $\dfrac{a}{b}$ is $\dfrac{b}{a}$ (flip numerator and denominator). To divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal:

$$\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc}$$

Memory aid: KCF — Keep, Change, Flip. Keep the first fraction, Change $\div$ to $\times$, Flip the second fraction.

Example 2.6 — Dividing Fractions and Mixed Numbers

(a) $\dfrac{5}{6} \div \dfrac{2}{3}$

$$\frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{15}{12} = \frac{5}{4} = 1\frac{1}{4}$$

(b) $4\dfrac{1}{2} \div 1\dfrac{1}{3}$

Convert: $\dfrac{9}{2} \div \dfrac{4}{3} = \dfrac{9}{2} \times \dfrac{3}{4} = \dfrac{27}{8} = 3\dfrac{3}{8}$
Answer (b): $3\dfrac{3}{8}$

2.7 Decimals and Place Value

A decimal number uses place value positions to the right of the decimal point: tenths ($10^{-1}$), hundredths ($10^{-2}$), thousandths ($10^{-3}$), and so on.

2.8 Converting Between Fractions and Decimals

Conversion Methods

Fraction → Decimal: Divide the numerator by the denominator.

$$\frac{3}{8} = 3 \div 8 = 0.375 \quad (\text{terminating})$$

Decimal → Fraction: Write the decimal over the appropriate power of 10, then simplify.

$$0.35 = \frac{35}{100} = \frac{7}{20}$$

Repeating Decimals

Some fractions produce decimals that repeat a pattern forever. We use a bar over the repeating block: $\dfrac{1}{3} = 0.\overline{3}$ and $\dfrac{5}{11} = 0.\overline{45}$.

Example 2.7 — Converting a Repeating Decimal to a Fraction

Convert $0.\overline{27}$ to a fraction.

Step 1: Let $x = 0.\overline{27} = 0.272727\ldots$
Step 2: The repeating block has 2 digits, so multiply by $100$:
$100x = 27.272727\ldots$
Step 3: Subtract the original equation:
$100x - x = 27.272727\ldots - 0.272727\ldots$
$99x = 27$
Step 4: Solve: $x = \dfrac{27}{99} = \dfrac{3}{11}$
Answer: $0.\overline{27} = \dfrac{3}{11}$

A fraction $\frac{p}{q}$ (in lowest terms) produces a terminating decimal if and only if $q$ has no prime factors other than 2 and 5.

Example 2.8 — Real-World Fractions

A recipe calls for $2\dfrac{3}{4}$ cups of flour and $1\dfrac{1}{3}$ cups of sugar. How much total dry ingredient is needed? If the recipe is doubled, how much flour is used?

Total: $2\dfrac{3}{4} + 1\dfrac{1}{3}$. LCD(4,3) = 12.
$\dfrac{11}{4} + \dfrac{4}{3} = \dfrac{33}{12} + \dfrac{16}{12} = \dfrac{49}{12} = 4\dfrac{1}{12}$ cups
Doubled flour: $2 \times 2\dfrac{3}{4} = 2 \times \dfrac{11}{4} = \dfrac{22}{4} = \dfrac{11}{2} = 5\dfrac{1}{2}$ cups
Answer: Total dry ingredient: $4\dfrac{1}{12}$ cups; doubled flour: $5\dfrac{1}{2}$ cups.

Example 2.9 — Dividing a Quantity

A board $7\dfrac{1}{2}$ feet long is cut into pieces each $\dfrac{3}{4}$ foot long. How many pieces can be cut?

$7\dfrac{1}{2} \div \dfrac{3}{4} = \dfrac{15}{2} \div \dfrac{3}{4} = \dfrac{15}{2} \times \dfrac{4}{3} = \dfrac{60}{6} = 10$
Answer: 10 pieces

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Adding denominators: $\dfrac{1}{3} + \dfrac{1}{4} \neq \dfrac{2}{7}$. You must find a common denominator first.
  • Forgetting to convert mixed numbers: Always convert to improper fractions before multiplying or dividing mixed numbers.
  • Simplifying after multiplication: It is fine to simplify after, but cross-canceling before is more efficient.
  • Dividing — flip the right fraction: In $\dfrac{a}{b} \div \dfrac{c}{d}$, flip $\dfrac{c}{d}$, not $\dfrac{a}{b}$.

Chapter Summary

  • Fractions come in three forms: proper ($a < b$), improper ($a \geq b$), and mixed numbers.
  • To simplify, divide both terms by their GCF; to find common denominators, use the LCM.
  • Adding/Subtracting: Requires a common denominator.
  • Multiplying: Multiply numerators and denominators directly; cross-cancel first when possible.
  • Dividing: Keep, Change, Flip — multiply by the reciprocal.
  • A fraction produces a terminating decimal if its denominator (in lowest terms) has only factors of 2 and 5; otherwise, the decimal repeats.
  • To convert a repeating decimal to a fraction, use the algebraic method: multiply by a power of 10 and subtract.
