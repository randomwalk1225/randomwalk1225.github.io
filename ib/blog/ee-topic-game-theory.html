IB EE Topic: Game Theory and Economic Decision Making

March 2026 · MathHub IB
← Back to IB Blog

Game theory occupies a unique position in the landscape of IB Extended Essay topics. It is mathematics with a human face — a rigorous formal framework for analyzing strategic interaction between rational decision makers. For students who find pure mathematics too abstract and pure economics too descriptive, game theory offers the perfect middle ground: precise mathematical reasoning applied to questions about human behavior, cooperation, competition, and the evolution of social norms.

Having supervised game theory Extended Essays in both Mathematics and Economics, I can attest that this topic consistently produces engaged students and strong essays. The mathematical framework is accessible to HL students (payoff matrices, probability, optimization), the key results are profound and counterintuitive (rational agents can reach collectively irrational outcomes), and the experimental component — running computer tournaments or analyzing real-world strategic scenarios — provides abundant material for analysis.

Why Game Theory Makes a Compelling EE

Game theory satisfies the Extended Essay criteria in ways that few other topics can. The mathematical background includes matrix algebra, probability theory, and optimization — all within the IB Math AA HL syllabus (Criterion B). The methodology can combine formal mathematical proofs with computational experiments, demonstrating both analytical and practical skills (Criterion C). And the applications to economics, biology, political science, and everyday life provide rich material for critical evaluation and personal engagement (Criterion D).

The topic also has a strong narrative dimension. Game theory was developed by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern in the 1940s, formalized by John Nash in the 1950s, and revolutionized by Robert Axelrod's computer tournaments in the 1980s. Each of these milestones produced insights that changed how economists, biologists, and political scientists think about strategic behavior. Weaving this intellectual history into your essay gives it context and depth that examiners appreciate.

Recommended Research Questions

Primary recommendation: "How does the iterated prisoner's dilemma demonstrate the evolution of cooperative behavior in competitive environments?"

This question is excellent because it targets a specific, well-studied game (the iterated prisoner's dilemma), identifies a precise phenomenon to investigate (the emergence of cooperation), and can be explored through both mathematical analysis and computer simulation. The word "how" invites a mechanistic explanation, not just a yes/no answer.

Alternative research questions:

  • "How does the introduction of noise (random errors) in the iterated prisoner's dilemma affect the relative success of different strategies?"
  • "To what extent does Nash equilibrium predict actual human behavior in experimental public goods games?"
  • "How do mixed strategy Nash equilibria explain observed behavior in competitive sports (penalty kicks, tennis serves)?"
  • "Under what conditions does the tragedy of the commons arise in common-pool resource games, and how do institutional rules prevent it?"

Key Concepts You Need to Master

Normal Form Games and Payoff Matrices

A game in normal (strategic) form is defined by three elements: a set of players, a set of strategies available to each player, and a payoff function that assigns a numerical outcome to each combination of strategies. For two-player games, this is typically represented as a payoff matrix. Consider the classic prisoner's dilemma:

Player B: CooperatePlayer B: Defect
Player A: Cooperate(3, 3)(0, 5)
Player A: Defect(5, 0)(1, 1)

In this payoff matrix, the first number in each pair is Player A's payoff and the second is Player B's. The dilemma is that mutual cooperation (3, 3) is better for both players than mutual defection (1, 1), yet each player has a dominant strategy to defect — regardless of what the other player does, defecting yields a higher payoff. This tension between individual rationality and collective welfare is the central theme of game theory and the foundation of your essay.

Nash Equilibrium

A Nash equilibrium is a set of strategies — one for each player — where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy. In the prisoner's dilemma, (Defect, Defect) is the unique Nash equilibrium. Neither player can do better by switching to Cooperate while the other Defects. Nash's theorem (1950) proves that every finite game has at least one Nash equilibrium, possibly in mixed strategies (where players randomize). In your essay, you should formally define Nash equilibrium, identify it in your chosen game, and discuss what it means — and what it does not mean. A Nash equilibrium is not necessarily the best outcome for the players; it is simply a stable one.

Dominant Strategies and Mixed Strategies

A dominant strategy is one that yields a higher payoff than any alternative regardless of what other players do. In the prisoner's dilemma, Defect is dominant for both players. Many games, however, have no dominant strategy. In matching pennies (where one player wants to match and the other wants to mismatch), the unique Nash equilibrium is a mixed strategy where each player randomizes 50-50 between Heads and Tails. Mixed strategies are particularly interesting because they predict behavior in competitive sports — research has shown that professional soccer players' penalty kick patterns closely match the mixed strategy Nash equilibrium prediction.

The Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma

The single-round prisoner's dilemma always leads to mutual defection, which seems to doom cooperation. But when the same two players meet repeatedly with no fixed endpoint, cooperation can emerge. The reason is reputation: in an iterated game, a player who defects today may face retaliation tomorrow. This transforms the incentive structure entirely. Analyzing how different strategies perform over hundreds of repeated rounds is the core of Axelrod's tournament approach and the most fertile ground for your essay.

Axelrod's Tournaments and Tit-for-Tat

In 1980, political scientist Robert Axelrod invited game theorists to submit strategies for a computer tournament of the iterated prisoner's dilemma. Each strategy played 200 rounds against every other strategy, and total scores were compared. The winner was the simplest strategy submitted: Tit-for-Tat (TFT), which cooperates on the first move and then copies whatever the opponent did in the previous round. TFT succeeds because it is "nice" (never defects first), "retaliatory" (punishes defection immediately), "forgiving" (returns to cooperation after the opponent does), and "clear" (its behavior is easy for other strategies to understand and predict).

Axelrod's result was profound: in a world of competitive, self-interested agents, the most successful strategy was fundamentally cooperative. This finding has implications for economics, evolutionary biology, international relations, and any domain where repeated interactions occur. Discussing and testing this result is the heart of a game theory EE.

Methodology: Running Your Own Tournament

I strongly recommend replicating and extending Axelrod's tournament as the experimental component of your essay. Here is a structured approach:

  1. Implement the tournament framework: In Python, create a system where strategies can play iterated prisoner's dilemma rounds against each other. Each strategy is a function that takes the history of previous moves and returns Cooperate or Defect.
  2. Code a diverse set of strategies: Include at least 8-10 strategies:
    • Always Cooperate: cooperates unconditionally
    • Always Defect: defects unconditionally
    • Tit-for-Tat: copies opponent's last move
    • Generous Tit-for-Tat: like TFT but cooperates with 10% probability even after opponent defects
    • Suspicious Tit-for-Tat: defects first, then copies
    • Pavlov (Win-Stay, Lose-Shift): repeats last action if payoff was high, switches if low
    • Random: cooperates or defects with equal probability
    • Grudger: cooperates until opponent defects once, then defects forever
  3. Run round-robin tournaments: Each strategy plays every other strategy for 200 rounds. Record total payoffs. Repeat with different random seeds for stochastic strategies.
  4. Vary tournament parameters: Change the number of rounds (50, 100, 200, 500), the payoff values, and the strategy mix. Analyze how results change.
  5. Introduce noise: Add a small probability (1-5%) that a player's intended action is flipped (cooperate becomes defect or vice versa). This simulates real-world miscommunication and dramatically changes which strategies succeed.
  6. Evolutionary dynamics: Run an evolutionary tournament where successful strategies "reproduce" (increase in population) and unsuccessful ones "die out." Track population composition over 100+ generations. Does cooperation evolve?
Common pitfall: Students often report tournament results without analysis. "Tit-for-Tat won" is a fact, not an insight. You need to explain why TFT won — which properties made it successful, which opponents it beat and lost to, and how results changed when you varied parameters. The depth of your analysis is what earns marks, not the complexity of your code.

Famous Results and Their Implications

Your essay should discuss how game theory illuminates real-world phenomena. The tragedy of the commons — where individual exploitation of a shared resource leads to collective ruin — is a multi-player prisoner's dilemma. Hardin's classic example of overgrazing a common pasture maps directly onto the payoff structure you analyze. Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize-winning work showed that real communities often solve this dilemma through institutional rules, paralleling how tournament rules (noise, repetition, reputation) enable cooperation in your simulations.

In evolutionary biology, the prisoner's dilemma explains the evolution of altruistic behavior. Organisms that cooperate (at a personal cost) can outcompete defectors when interactions are repeated and recognition is possible. Your evolutionary tournament directly simulates this process. The mathematical connection between game theory and natural selection — formalized by John Maynard Smith's concept of evolutionarily stable strategies — is a powerful extension for your essay.

Structuring Your Extended Essay

  1. Introduction (400 words): research question, motivation, historical context of game theory
  2. Mathematical Background (800 words): normal form games, Nash equilibrium, dominant strategies, iterated games
  3. Methodology (500 words): tournament design, strategy descriptions, parameter choices
  4. Results and Analysis (1100 words): tournament outcomes, parameter variation effects, noise analysis, evolutionary dynamics
  5. Discussion (700 words): real-world implications, limitations of the model, comparison with human experiments
  6. Conclusion (400 words): answer the research question, reflect on cooperation and rationality

Game theory is one of those topics where the mathematics genuinely changes how you see the world. An EE on this topic is not just an academic exercise — it is an invitation to think more carefully about cooperation, competition, and the structures that shape human interaction. That intellectual excitement, when it comes through in your writing, is what makes the difference between a competent essay and a memorable one.
