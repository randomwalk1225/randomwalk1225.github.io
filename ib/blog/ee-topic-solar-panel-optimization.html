IB EE Topic: Mathematical Modeling of Solar Panel Efficiency

March 2026 · MathHub IB
← Back to IB Blog

Solar energy is one of the defining challenges of our generation, and the mathematics behind solar panel optimization is both beautiful and practical. For IB students seeking an Extended Essay topic that combines trigonometry, calculus, and real-world engineering, modeling solar panel efficiency offers an exceptional opportunity. The core question — at what angle should a solar panel be tilted to capture the most energy? — is deceptively simple. Answering it rigorously requires understanding Earth's orbital mechanics, atmospheric physics, and optimization techniques that form the backbone of IB Mathematics HL.

What I find compelling about this topic, having guided students through it multiple times, is that it rewards genuine mathematical reasoning. You cannot copy an answer from Wikipedia because the optimal angle depends on latitude, and your essay should derive the result for a specific location using first principles. Examiners can immediately see whether a student has done authentic mathematical work or merely reported someone else's findings.

Why Solar Panel Optimization Works as an EE

This topic excels across the EE assessment criteria. The mathematical background requires trigonometric modeling and calculus-based optimization — core IB Math AA HL skills applied to a novel context (Criterion B). The methodology involves building a mathematical model, making predictions, and comparing them with real solar irradiance data — a structured scientific approach (Criterion C). And the applications to renewable energy provide genuine motivation and opportunities for critical reflection on the model's limitations (Criterion D).

Solar panel optimization also avoids a common EE trap: being too abstract. Unlike purely theoretical topics, this one produces a concrete, testable answer (an optimal tilt angle in degrees) that you can verify against published engineering data and real-world solar installations. That connection between theory and practice is exactly what examiners want to see.

Recommended Research Questions

Primary recommendation: "What is the optimal fixed tilt angle for a solar panel at latitude 37°N to maximize annual energy output?"

This question is strong because it specifies a latitude (making the problem concrete and personal — choose your own city), focuses on a fixed tilt (keeping the scope manageable), and targets annual energy (requiring integration over seasonal variations). The answer is not trivially "tilt equals latitude" — atmospheric effects, seasonal weighting, and the nonlinear relationship between angle and energy make the optimization genuinely interesting.

Alternative research questions:

  • "How does seasonal adjustment of solar panel tilt angle (e.g., changing tilt four times per year) compare to a fixed optimal tilt in terms of total annual energy capture?"
  • "To what extent does atmospheric attenuation (Beer-Lambert law) modify the theoretically optimal tilt angle for solar panels?"
  • "How does the mathematical model for optimal solar panel orientation compare with empirical solar irradiance data from weather stations at latitude 52°N?"

Key Concepts for Your Mathematical Model

Solar Declination Angle

The solar declination angle is the angle between the Sun's rays and the plane of Earth's equator. It varies throughout the year because Earth's axis is tilted at 23.44 degrees relative to its orbital plane. The declination can be approximated by the formula: delta = 23.44 * sin(360/365 * (284 + d)) degrees, where d is the day of the year (1-365). This function oscillates between +23.44 degrees (summer solstice in the Northern Hemisphere) and -23.44 degrees (winter solstice). In your essay, you should derive or justify this formula and plot it over one year to show the seasonal variation.

Hour Angle and Solar Altitude

The hour angle describes the Sun's east-west position in the sky relative to the local meridian. At solar noon, the hour angle is zero; it changes by 15 degrees per hour (since 360/24 = 15). The solar altitude angle — the Sun's angle above the horizon — depends on the observer's latitude (phi), the solar declination (delta), and the hour angle (omega). The formula is: sin(alpha) = sin(phi)*sin(delta) + cos(phi)*cos(delta)*cos(omega). This equation is the mathematical heart of your model. It tells you exactly where the Sun is in the sky at any time of any day at any latitude.

Angle of Incidence

The energy captured by a solar panel is proportional to the cosine of the angle between the incoming sunlight and the panel's surface normal (the direction the panel faces). For a south-facing panel tilted at angle beta from horizontal at latitude phi, the angle of incidence theta satisfies: cos(theta) = sin(alpha)*cos(beta) + cos(alpha)*sin(beta)*cos(azimuth), where alpha is the solar altitude and the azimuth is the Sun's horizontal direction. The key insight is that energy capture is maximized when theta = 0 (sunlight hits the panel perpendicularly), but since the Sun moves throughout the day and across seasons, no fixed tilt can achieve theta = 0 at all times. Finding the tilt that minimizes the average angle of incidence — or equivalently, maximizes the average cos(theta) — is your optimization problem.

Atmospheric Attenuation: Beer-Lambert Law

Sunlight passing through Earth's atmosphere is attenuated by absorption and scattering. The Beer-Lambert law models this: I = I_0 * e^(-k * AM), where I_0 is the extraterrestrial solar irradiance (approximately 1361 W/m^2), k is an atmospheric extinction coefficient, and AM is the air mass — the relative path length through the atmosphere. Air mass depends on the solar altitude: AM = 1/sin(alpha) for alpha greater than about 10 degrees. When the Sun is low in the sky, light travels through more atmosphere and is more attenuated. This effect is crucial because it means winter sunlight (when the Sun is low) is weaker per unit area than summer sunlight, which shifts the optimal tilt angle compared to a naive geometric calculation.

Integration for Total Annual Energy

The total energy captured over a year is the integral of instantaneous power over all daylight hours of all 365 days. In practice, you discretize this: for each day d from 1 to 365, and for each hour of daylight, calculate the solar altitude, the angle of incidence on the tilted panel, and the atmospheric attenuation. Sum the instantaneous irradiance values (keeping only positive values — the Sun must be above the horizon and in front of the panel) to get the total annual energy E(beta) as a function of tilt angle beta. Then find the value of beta that maximizes E(beta). This is a numerical optimization problem that you can solve in Python by computing E(beta) for beta ranging from 0 to 90 degrees in 1-degree increments and finding the maximum.

Methodology: Building and Validating Your Model

  1. Choose a location: Select a city whose latitude is meaningful to you. Record the latitude (phi) precisely. For latitude 37°N, possible cities include Seoul, San Francisco, Athens, or Seville.
  2. Implement the solar geometry model: In Python, code the declination formula, the solar altitude equation, and the angle of incidence calculation. Validate by checking that your model predicts sunrise and sunset times that match published data for your location.
  3. Add atmospheric attenuation: Implement the Beer-Lambert law with a reasonable extinction coefficient (k ≈ 0.1 to 0.3 depending on atmospheric clarity).
  4. Compute annual energy vs. tilt angle: Loop over tilt angles from 0° to 90°, computing total annual energy for each. Plot E(beta) and identify the optimal angle.
  5. Sensitivity analysis: Vary the atmospheric extinction coefficient and observe how the optimal angle changes. This demonstrates critical thinking about model assumptions.
  6. Validate against real data: Compare your model's predictions with published solar irradiance data from weather stations or databases like NASA POWER or PVGIS. Discuss discrepancies — clouds, terrain, albedo effects that your model does not account for.
Common pitfall: Many students state "the optimal tilt angle equals the latitude" without doing the calculation. This rule of thumb is approximately correct for simple geometric models but breaks down when you include atmospheric attenuation, which penalizes low solar angles more heavily. Your model should derive the optimal angle from first principles and show whether or not it matches the rule of thumb.

Expected Results

For a location at 37°N, your model should find an optimal fixed tilt angle somewhere in the range of 30° to 40°, depending on atmospheric conditions. The interesting finding is typically that the optimal angle is slightly less than the latitude — atmospheric attenuation makes summer energy (when the Sun is high) relatively more valuable, favoring a slightly flatter panel that captures more summer radiation. This nuanced result, derived from your own model, is exactly the kind of analysis that earns top marks.

If you extend your analysis to seasonal adjustment (e.g., steeper tilt in winter, flatter in summer), you should find that even a simple two-angle strategy (winter tilt and summer tilt) captures 5-10% more annual energy than the fixed optimum. Quantifying this improvement gives your essay a practical engineering dimension.

Structuring Your Extended Essay

  1. Introduction (400 words): research question, motivation from renewable energy context, scope
  2. Mathematical Background (900 words): solar geometry, trigonometric formulas, Beer-Lambert law, optimization setup
  3. Methodology (500 words): computational approach, validation strategy, data sources
  4. Results (1000 words): energy vs. tilt plots, optimal angle determination, sensitivity analysis, seasonal comparison
  5. Discussion (700 words): validation against real data, model limitations, engineering implications
  6. Conclusion (400 words): answer the research question, reflect on the power of mathematical modeling

This topic lets you showcase trigonometry, calculus, and numerical methods in a context that matters. It is the kind of essay that makes an examiner think: this student truly understands how mathematics connects to the real world.
