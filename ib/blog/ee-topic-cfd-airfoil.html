Why CFD Is an Impressive EE Topic

Computational Fluid Dynamics sits at the intersection of physics, mathematics, and computer science. It involves solving the Navier-Stokes equations — a system of nonlinear partial differential equations that governs fluid motion — using numerical methods on a discretized computational domain. For a student aiming at engineering, physics, or applied mathematics at university, a CFD-based Extended Essay demonstrates exactly the kind of interdisciplinary thinking that admissions tutors value.

The specific case of airflow over an airfoil is a classic problem in aerodynamics. The NACA (National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics) series of airfoil profiles have been extensively studied experimentally since the 1930s, which means abundant validation data exists. You can simulate the flow around a NACA 2412 airfoil at various angles of attack, compute the lift and drag coefficients, and compare your results directly against published experimental data from NASA wind tunnel tests.

What makes this topic stand out is the level of intellectual ambition. Most IB students write Extended Essays on topics they can handle comfortably. A CFD essay announces that you can handle partial differential equations, mesh generation, numerical convergence, and scientific computing — topics that many university students encounter for the first time in their second or third year. Done well, this is an A-grade essay that also serves as a portfolio piece for university applications.

Honesty check: This is one of the most demanding EE topics you can choose. If you do not have prior experience with programming (Python at minimum) and are not comfortable learning new software independently, consider a different topic. The learning curve for CFD tools is steep, and you need to allocate significant time (at least 15-20 hours) just for software setup and familiarization before you begin your actual investigation.

Sample Research Questions

  • "How does the angle of attack affect the lift and drag coefficients on a NACA 2412 airfoil as predicted by CFD simulation, and how do these predictions compare with experimental wind tunnel data?" — The gold standard question. Clear, specific, measurable. Allows you to vary one parameter (angle of attack from 0 to 15 degrees) and analyze two output quantities (lift coefficient CL and drag coefficient CD).
  • "At what angle of attack does flow separation (stall) occur on a NACA 0012 airfoil at Reynolds number 500,000, and how does the CFD prediction compare with experimental observations?" — Focused on a specific phenomenon (stall). The NACA 0012 is symmetric, which simplifies some aspects of the analysis.
  • "How does mesh refinement affect the accuracy and convergence of CFD-predicted lift coefficients for a NACA 2412 airfoil at 5 degrees angle of attack?" — A numerics-focused question. You systematically refine the mesh and observe how the solution converges. Excellent for demonstrating understanding of numerical methods.
  • "To what extent can a simplified 2D panel method predict the lift coefficient of a NACA 4412 airfoil compared to a full Navier-Stokes CFD simulation?" — Compares two computational approaches. The panel method is simpler to implement from scratch in Python, while CFD requires specialized software.

Key Concepts: The Mathematics of Fluid Flow

The Navier-Stokes Equations

The Navier-Stokes equations are the governing equations of fluid motion. For incompressible, viscous flow (appropriate for low-speed aerodynamics), they consist of the momentum equation and the continuity equation. In two dimensions, the momentum equations state that the rate of change of velocity equals the sum of pressure forces, viscous forces, and external body forces per unit mass.

You do not need to derive the Navier-Stokes equations from first principles in your essay, but you must demonstrate that you understand what each term represents physically: the convective acceleration term (nonlinear, responsible for turbulence), the pressure gradient term (drives flow from high to low pressure), and the viscous diffusion term (resistance due to fluid viscosity).

Reynolds Number

The Reynolds number, Re = ρvL/μ, is a dimensionless quantity that characterizes the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces in the flow. Here ρ is the fluid density, v is the freestream velocity, L is the characteristic length (chord length for an airfoil), and μ is the dynamic viscosity. At low Reynolds numbers (Re < 2000 for pipe flow), the flow is laminar and smooth. At high Reynolds numbers (Re > 500,000 for airfoils), the flow becomes turbulent and requires different modeling approaches.

For your essay, selecting an appropriate Reynolds number is critical. Most NACA experimental data was collected at Re between 3 million and 9 million. If your simulation uses a different Reynolds number, your results will not match the experimental data, and your entire validation becomes meaningless. State your Reynolds number explicitly and justify your choice.

Lift and Drag Coefficients

The lift coefficient CL = L / (0.5 × ρ × v² × A) and drag coefficient CD = D / (0.5 × ρ × v² × A) are dimensionless measures of aerodynamic performance. L is the lift force (perpendicular to freestream), D is the drag force (parallel to freestream), and A is the reference area (chord length times span for 2D simulations). These coefficients allow comparison between simulations at different scales and conditions.

Boundary Layers and Flow Separation

Near the airfoil surface, the no-slip condition (fluid velocity equals zero at the wall) creates a thin boundary layer where the velocity transitions from zero to the freestream value. At high angles of attack, the adverse pressure gradient on the upper surface causes the boundary layer to separate from the airfoil, resulting in a dramatic loss of lift called stall. Capturing this separation accurately is one of the key challenges in CFD and a rich source of analysis for your essay.

Mesh Generation

CFD solves the Navier-Stokes equations on a discrete computational mesh — a grid of cells covering the domain around the airfoil. The accuracy of the solution depends critically on the mesh quality: cells must be small enough near the airfoil surface to resolve the boundary layer, but can be larger far from the airfoil where gradients are small. A mesh refinement study — running the same simulation on progressively finer meshes and observing convergence — is essential for establishing the credibility of your results.

Key insight for your essay: The mathematical content of a CFD essay is not just "I ran a simulation." It is the systematic analysis of how discretization choices (mesh size, solver settings, turbulence model) affect the accuracy and convergence of the numerical solution. This is where you demonstrate critical thinking.

Methodology: Simulation and Validation

  1. Define the geometry: Select a NACA airfoil profile (2412 is a good default — cambered, well-documented). Generate the airfoil coordinates using the NACA 4-digit formula or download them from airfoiltools.com.
  2. Generate the mesh: Create a computational domain extending at least 10 chord lengths from the airfoil in all directions. Refine the mesh near the airfoil surface (first cell height calculated from your target y+ value for the chosen turbulence model).
  3. Set boundary conditions: Freestream velocity at the inlet, pressure outlet at the exit, no-slip walls on the airfoil surface, symmetry or far-field conditions on the outer boundaries.
  4. Run simulations: Solve at angles of attack from 0 to 15 degrees in 1-degree increments. For each case, monitor residual convergence to ensure the solution is converged before extracting force coefficients.
  5. Mesh refinement study: Run at least three mesh densities (coarse, medium, fine) at one angle of attack. Plot CL versus mesh cell count to demonstrate convergence. This is non-negotiable for a credible CFD study.
  6. Validation: Compare your CL and CD versus angle of attack curves against published experimental data (Abbott and von Doenhoff, 1959, is the standard reference for NACA airfoils).
  7. Analysis: Quantify the agreement (percentage error, trend comparison). Discuss where and why your CFD results deviate from experiment. Common reasons include turbulence model limitations, 2D vs. 3D effects, and insufficient mesh resolution in the boundary layer.

How to Structure This EE

  1. Introduction (400 words): Importance of aerodynamics, role of CFD in modern engineering, research question, overview of approach.
  2. Theoretical Background (800 words): Navier-Stokes equations (state and explain, do not derive from scratch), Reynolds number, lift and drag coefficients, boundary layers, NACA airfoil geometry.
  3. Methodology (700 words): Software selection justification, mesh generation process, boundary conditions, solver settings, mesh refinement strategy.
  4. Results (900 words): CL and CD versus angle of attack plots, pressure distribution visualizations, flow field images showing streamlines and velocity contours, mesh convergence data.
  5. Validation and Discussion (700 words): Comparison with experimental data, error analysis, discussion of discrepancies, limitations of the simulation (turbulence model, 2D assumption, mesh resolution).
  6. Conclusion (500 words): Direct answer to research question, summary of key findings, what the investigation reveals about the capabilities and limitations of CFD for aerodynamic prediction.

Expected Grade Potential

A well-executed CFD essay is a strong A candidate. University admissions tutors will be genuinely impressed by a 17-year-old who can set up and analyze a CFD simulation. The mathematical content is sophisticated, the methodology is rigorous, and the comparison with experimental data provides a natural framework for critical analysis.

However, this topic has a higher failure risk than simpler topics. If the software does not work, if the mesh does not converge, or if the validation data does not match, you can end up with an essay that has a lot of effort but insufficient results to analyze. Start the computational work early — ideally over the summer — and have a fallback plan. If full CFD proves too difficult, a panel method implementation in Python can still earn an A with strong mathematical analysis.

University application advantage: This topic is particularly strong for students applying to Aeronautical Engineering, Mechanical Engineering, or Applied Mathematics. Imperial College, MIT, Stanford, and ETH Zurich all have strong CFD research groups, and mentioning your EE topic at interview immediately signals serious interest and capability.
