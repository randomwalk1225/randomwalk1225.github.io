IB EE Topic: A* Pathfinding Algorithm — Analysis and Implementation

March 2026 · MathHub IB
← Back to IB Blog

Pathfinding algorithms sit at a remarkable intersection of mathematics and computer science. They are elegant enough to analyze theoretically, practical enough to implement and test, and significant enough to produce meaningful Extended Essay conclusions. Having supervised dozens of IB Extended Essays on algorithmic topics, I can say with confidence that the A* pathfinding algorithm is one of the strongest choices a student can make for a CS or Mathematics EE.

What makes pathfinding particularly compelling is that the core problem is simple to state — find the shortest route between two points — yet the solution space is rich with mathematical depth. You can explore graph theory, analyze computational complexity, compare heuristic functions, and produce quantitative experimental data, all within a single well-scoped essay.

Why Pathfinding Algorithms Excel as an EE Topic

The IB Extended Essay assessment criteria reward depth of analysis, personal engagement, and critical evaluation. Pathfinding algorithms deliver on all three fronts. The mathematical framework behind A* draws on graph theory, which most IB Math AA HL students encounter in some form. The algorithm itself is deterministic and reproducible, meaning your experiments will produce consistent, analyzable results. And the real-world applications — from GPS navigation to video game AI to robotic motion planning — provide natural motivation for your research question.

Examiners consistently reward essays that demonstrate genuine understanding rather than surface-level description. With A*, you can show understanding by deriving why the algorithm is optimal under certain conditions, by proving that admissible heuristics guarantee shortest paths, and by analyzing the trade-offs between computational speed and solution quality. This kind of mathematical reasoning is exactly what earns marks in Criterion C (Critical Thinking).

Recommended Research Questions

The most common mistake students make with algorithmic EEs is choosing a research question that is either too broad ("How do pathfinding algorithms work?") or too narrow ("Does A* work?"). Your question should be investigative and measurable. Here are strong options:

Primary recommendation: "How does the choice of heuristic function in the A* algorithm affect pathfinding efficiency in grid-based environments?"

This question is strong because it has a clear independent variable (the heuristic function), measurable dependent variables (nodes expanded, computation time, path length), and enough depth to sustain 4,000 words of analysis.

Alternative research questions worth considering:

  • "To what extent does obstacle density in a grid environment affect the relative performance advantage of A* over Dijkstra's algorithm?"
  • "How does grid resolution affect the trade-off between path optimality and computational cost in A* pathfinding?"
  • "Under what conditions does A* with an inadmissible heuristic produce significantly suboptimal paths, and how does the degree of inadmissibility correlate with path quality?"

Key Concepts You Need to Master

Graph Theory Foundations

At its core, pathfinding operates on graphs — structures consisting of nodes (vertices) connected by edges. A grid-based map is simply a graph where each cell is a node and adjacent cells share edges. Understanding this abstraction is essential because it lets you apply A* to any domain, not just grids. In your EE, you should clearly explain this connection and demonstrate that you understand the mathematical structure underlying the algorithm.

The A* Algorithm

A* evaluates nodes using the function f(n) = g(n) + h(n), where g(n) is the exact cost from the start node to node n, and h(n) is a heuristic estimate of the cost from n to the goal. The algorithm maintains an open set (nodes to explore) and a closed set (nodes already explored), always expanding the node with the lowest f(n) value. This greedy-best-first approach, guided by the heuristic, is what gives A* its efficiency advantage over uninformed search algorithms like BFS and Dijkstra.

Heuristic Functions

The heuristic is where most of your analysis will focus. Three common heuristics for grid-based pathfinding are:

HeuristicFormulaBest For
Manhattan Distance|x1 - x2| + |y1 - y2|4-directional movement (no diagonals)
Euclidean Distancesqrt((x1-x2)^2 + (y1-y2)^2)Any-angle movement
Chebyshev Distancemax(|x1-x2|, |y1-y2|)8-directional movement (with diagonals)

A heuristic is admissible if it never overestimates the true cost to the goal. When A* uses an admissible heuristic, it is guaranteed to find the optimal (shortest) path. Manhattan distance is admissible for 4-directional grids; Chebyshev is admissible for 8-directional grids. Euclidean distance is admissible for both. Understanding and proving admissibility is a strong mathematical component for your essay.

Time Complexity

The worst-case time complexity of A* is O(bd), where b is the branching factor and d is the depth of the solution. However, the effective complexity depends heavily on the quality of the heuristic. A perfect heuristic (one that always returns the exact remaining cost) would make A* expand only the nodes on the optimal path, achieving O(d) complexity. This gap between worst-case and best-case behavior is a rich area for analysis in your essay.

A* vs. Dijkstra vs. BFS

Dijkstra's algorithm is essentially A* with h(n) = 0 — it uses no heuristic and explores in all directions equally. BFS finds the shortest path in unweighted graphs but cannot handle varying edge costs. Comparing these three algorithms experimentally, measuring nodes expanded and computation time on identical grids, gives you strong quantitative data for your essay. You should expect to find that A* explores significantly fewer nodes than Dijkstra in most scenarios, with the advantage growing as grid size increases.

Methodology: Building Your Experiment

Your experimental methodology should follow a controlled, scientific approach. I recommend Python as the implementation language because it is accessible, well-documented, and has excellent visualization libraries.

Implementation Steps

  1. Build a grid generator that creates n x n grids with configurable obstacle density (typically 10% to 40% of cells blocked). Use a random seed for reproducibility.
  2. Implement A* with swappable heuristic functions. Track the number of nodes expanded, the final path length, and the wall-clock computation time for each run.
  3. Implement Dijkstra and BFS as baselines for comparison. Structurally, Dijkstra is A* with h(n) = 0, so the implementation is minimal.
  4. Design your experiments: vary grid sizes (20x20, 50x50, 100x100, 200x200), obstacle densities (10%, 20%, 30%), and heuristic functions. Run each configuration multiple times with different random seeds and report averages.
  5. Visualize results: generate path visualizations showing explored nodes (colored cells on the grid) and line graphs of nodes expanded vs. grid size for each algorithm.
Common pitfall: Many students implement the algorithm correctly but fail to collect enough data. Run at least 30 trials per configuration to produce statistically meaningful results. Report means and standard deviations, not single data points.

Data Analysis

Present your results in tables and graphs. Calculate the percentage reduction in nodes expanded when using A* versus Dijkstra. Analyze how this advantage changes with grid size and obstacle density. If you find anomalies — perhaps A* with Manhattan distance performs poorly on grids with diagonal movement — explain why mathematically. This kind of critical analysis, connecting experimental results back to theoretical properties, is what distinguishes a top-scoring EE from an average one.

Real-World Applications

Discussing applications demonstrates personal engagement and contextualizes your work. GPS navigation systems use variants of A* on road networks where edge weights represent travel time or distance. Video games use A* for NPC movement, often with optimizations like hierarchical pathfinding for large maps. Robotic motion planning extends pathfinding to continuous spaces with obstacles, using configuration-space representations. Even network routing protocols share conceptual foundations with A*. Choose one or two applications that genuinely interest you and discuss how the principles you analyzed apply at scale.

Structuring Your Extended Essay

A well-structured pathfinding EE typically follows this outline:

  1. Introduction (400 words): research question, motivation, scope
  2. Mathematical Background (800 words): graph theory, A* formulation, heuristic properties, admissibility proof
  3. Methodology (600 words): implementation details, experimental design, data collection plan
  4. Results and Analysis (1200 words): data presentation, tables, graphs, statistical analysis
  5. Discussion (600 words): interpretation, limitations, real-world connections
  6. Conclusion (400 words): answer the research question, reflect on findings

This structure ensures you stay within the 4,000-word limit while covering all assessment criteria. The bulk of your word count should be in Results and Analysis — that is where you demonstrate critical thinking and earn the highest marks.
