What is the Extended Essay?

The Extended Essay (EE) is one of three components of the IB Diploma core, alongside Theory of Knowledge (TOK) and the Creativity, Activity, Service (CAS) programme. It is a 4,000-word independent research paper that gives you the opportunity to investigate a topic of personal interest through the lens of one of your IB subjects.

The EE constitutes 20% of the Diploma core, and when combined with your TOK grade, it contributes up to 3 bonus points toward your total IB Diploma score. Those 3 points can be the difference between a 42 and a 45, or more practically, the difference between a university offer being met or missed.

Every IB Diploma candidate must submit an Extended Essay. You choose a subject from the IB curriculum (it does not need to be a subject you study at HL) and develop a focused research question. The essay is supervised by a teacher at your school who acts as a mentor, providing up to three formal reflection sessions and guidance throughout the process.

Key facts: The EE must not exceed 4,000 words (excluding abstract, bibliography, footnotes, and appendices). Going over the word limit is grounds for an automatic penalty. The IB expects approximately 40 hours of work spread over several months.

The Extended Essay is not a glorified homework assignment. It is the IB's way of assessing whether you can conduct sustained, independent academic research. Universities, particularly in the UK, take the EE seriously. Admissions tutors at Oxford and Cambridge routinely ask about EE topics at interview. A strong EE demonstrates intellectual maturity that grades alone cannot convey.

Assessment Criteria (34 Marks)

The EE is assessed against five criteria, totalling 34 marks. Understanding what each criterion rewards is essential for scoring an A.

CriterionWhat It AssessesMarks
A — Focus & MethodClear research question, appropriate methodology, source selection6
B — Knowledge & UnderstandingSubject-specific knowledge, context, academic credibility6
C — Critical ThinkingAnalysis, evaluation, argument development, discussion12
D — PresentationStructure, layout, referencing, formatting consistency4
E — EngagementEvidence of intellectual initiative and reflective process6
Criterion C (Critical Thinking) is worth 12 marks — more than one-third of the total. This is where most students lose the A grade. The IB wants to see you analyze evidence, weigh competing interpretations, identify limitations, and build a sustained argument. Simply describing or summarizing information, no matter how accurately, will cap you at a C or D on this criterion.

Criterion A — Focus & Method (6 marks): Your research question must be specific, clearly stated, and answerable within 4,000 words. Vague questions such as "What is the role of calculus in physics?" will score poorly. The methodology section should explain and justify your approach: Why did you choose this method? What are its strengths for your specific question?

Criterion B — Knowledge & Understanding (6 marks): You must demonstrate that you understand the academic context of your topic. For a Mathematics EE, this means citing relevant theorems, explaining concepts accurately, and showing awareness of where your work fits within the broader field. Using sources beyond your textbook is essential.

Criterion D — Presentation (4 marks): This is the easiest criterion to score full marks on, and the most frustrating to lose marks on. Use consistent formatting, proper headings, page numbers, a table of contents, and an accepted citation style (MLA, APA, or Chicago). Figures and tables should be numbered and referenced in the text.

Criterion E — Engagement (6 marks): Assessed through the Reflections on Planning and Progress Form (RPPF), which is a 500-word document completed with your supervisor. The IB wants evidence that you made intellectual decisions, adapted your approach when things did not work, and engaged authentically with the research process. Students who write the RPPF as an afterthought routinely lose 3-4 marks here.

Grade Boundaries and Diploma Points

The 34 raw marks translate into letter grades A through E. While exact boundaries shift slightly between examination sessions, the following ranges are typical:

GradeTypical Mark RangeWhat It Means
A28–34Excellent — publishable quality research
B22–27Good — solid work with minor weaknesses
C15–21Satisfactory — competent but lacks depth
D7–14Mediocre — significant weaknesses
E0–6Elementary — failing condition for diploma

The EE and TOK grades combine in a matrix to award 0 to 3 bonus points. An A in EE combined with an A in TOK gives 3 points. An A/B or B/A combination gives 3 points. A B/B gives 2 points. A D in either component gives 0 bonus points, and an E in either is a failing condition for the diploma — you will not receive your IB Diploma regardless of your subject scores.

Diploma failure condition: An E grade on either the EE or TOK results in automatic diploma denial, even if your total points from six subjects exceed 24. This is non-negotiable.

Step-by-Step Writing Process

1. Choose Your Subject and Topic Area

Start broad. If you are interested in mathematics, consider what areas excite you: pure mathematics (number theory, geometry, algebra), applied mathematics (modelling, statistics, optimization), or connections between mathematics and other fields (cryptography, physics, economics). Read widely before committing. Browse past EE titles on the IB's Exemplar Essays site and academic journals like the Mathematical Gazette or Plus Magazine for inspiration.

2. Formulate a Research Question

Your research question is the single most important sentence in your essay. It must be specific enough to answer in 4,000 words but substantial enough to warrant 40 hours of research. Test your question against these criteria: Can you identify what a complete answer would look like? Does it require analysis (not just description)? Is there genuine uncertainty in the answer?

Weak: "What is RSA encryption?" (descriptive, not analytical). Strong: "To what extent does the security of RSA encryption depend on the computational difficulty of integer factorization for semi-primes exceeding 2048 bits?"

3. Plan Your Methodology

For a Mathematics EE, methodology typically involves mathematical proof, computational modeling, data analysis, or a combination. Define your approach before you start writing. If you plan to use software (Python, MATLAB, GeoGebra), explain why that tool is appropriate. If you are comparing theoretical predictions with real data, specify your data sources and how you will measure agreement.

4. Conduct Research and Write Iteratively

Do not attempt to write the essay in a single draft. Begin with an extended outline (section headings, key arguments for each section, evidence you will use). Write the body sections first, then the introduction and conclusion. The introduction should be written last because only then do you know exactly what your essay argues.

5. Engage with the Reflection Process

Complete each of the three reflection sessions with your supervisor honestly. The RPPF is not a formality. Describe specific challenges you encountered, decisions you made, and how your thinking evolved. The IB's published descriptors for Criterion E explicitly reward "intellectual initiative" and "personal engagement with the process."

6. Revise, Proofread, and Format

Allow at least two weeks for revision. Check every mathematical expression for accuracy. Ensure all figures are numbered and referenced. Verify that your bibliography is complete and consistently formatted. Read the essay aloud to catch awkward phrasing. Ask someone unfamiliar with your topic to read it — if they cannot follow your argument, your structure needs work.

WhenMilestone

July–August (Summer)Conduct primary research, develop mathematical arguments, write first draft of body sections

Start early. Students who begin serious work over the summer between Year 1 and Year 2 consistently score higher than those who start in September. The summer provides uninterrupted time for deep research that the busy IB Year 2 schedule cannot offer.

Mathematics EE: Specific Advice

The Mathematics Extended Essay is one of the most rewarding but also one of the most challenging EE subjects. The IB reports that Mathematics EEs have a lower average grade than most other subjects, primarily because students choose topics that are either too ambitious or too descriptive.

Choose depth over breadth. A Mathematics EE that deeply investigates one well-defined question will always outperform an essay that surveys an entire field. "An exploration of the Mandelbrot set" is too broad. "How does the relationship between Julia sets and the Mandelbrot set illustrate the sensitivity of complex quadratic iteration to initial parameters?" is focused and assessable.

Your mathematics must be original in application, not in discovery. The IB does not expect you to prove new theorems. It expects you to apply known mathematics to a specific question in a way that demonstrates your understanding. You might apply the SIR model to a specific epidemic, use RSA encryption to analyze a particular key length, or compare numerical methods on a specific differential equation.

Show your working, but also explain your reasoning. A Mathematics EE that reads like a sequence of equations with no prose will score poorly on Criteria B and C. Every mathematical step should be accompanied by an explanation of why you are taking that step and what it reveals about your research question.

Use technology wisely. Computational tools (Python, MATLAB, Desmos, GeoGebra) are valuable for generating examples, testing conjectures, and creating visualizations. However, the essay must contain substantial mathematical reasoning — not just code output. If you run a simulation, explain the mathematics behind it. If you generate a graph, analyze what it shows.

Common Mathematics EE pitfall: Copying derivations from textbooks and presenting them as your own work. The IB calls this "descriptive" rather than "analytical" and it caps Criterion C at 4/12. Instead, take a known result and apply it to a new context, vary the parameters, test its limitations, or compare it with an alternative approach.

Common Mistakes That Cost You an A

  1. Research question too broad or too vague. If your question could serve as the title of a textbook chapter, it is too broad. Narrow it until the answer requires specific analysis, not a general survey.
  2. Insufficient critical thinking. Description earns marks on Criterion B but not on Criterion C. Every section of your essay should advance an argument. Ask yourself: "What am I proving, comparing, or evaluating here?"
  3. Neglecting the RPPF. Students treat the reflection form as administrative paperwork. It is worth 6 marks — the same as an entire criterion. Write it with the same care you give the essay itself.
  4. Exceeding the word limit. The 4,000-word limit is absolute. Examiners stop reading at 4,000 words. Any argument that appears after the limit is ignored. Plan your structure to say what you need within the constraint.
  5. Poor referencing. Every claim that is not your own original thought must be cited. In-text citations and a complete bibliography are not optional. The IB treats unreferenced claims as potential academic dishonesty.
  6. Leaving it too late. An EE written in two weeks over the Christmas break is almost guaranteed a C or lower. The 40-hour expectation exists because quality research takes sustained effort over months.
  7. Choosing a topic to impress rather than to investigate. Selecting a topic because it sounds advanced (quantum mechanics, Godel's incompleteness theorems) without having the background to investigate it properly is a reliable path to a D.
