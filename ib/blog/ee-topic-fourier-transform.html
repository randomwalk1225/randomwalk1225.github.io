IB EE Topic: Fourier Transform in Audio Signal Processing

March 2026 · MathHub IB
← Back to IB Blog

If you want your Extended Essay to make an examiner sit up and take notice, Fourier analysis is one of the strongest topics you can choose. It connects pure mathematics — trigonometric series, complex exponentials, integral transforms — to technology that students use every day: music streaming, voice recognition, noise cancellation, and medical imaging. The Fourier Transform is not just an abstract mathematical tool; it is the foundation of modern digital signal processing, and an EE exploring its application to audio signals can achieve the rare combination of mathematical rigor, practical experimentation, and genuine intellectual excitement.

From my experience supervising Extended Essays, Fourier analysis topics consistently score well because they naturally satisfy the assessment criteria. The mathematical background demands real understanding (Criterion B). The experimental component — recording audio, applying transforms, analyzing frequency spectra — requires personal engagement and methodological care (Criteria C and D). And the applications to real technology provide abundant material for critical evaluation.

Why Audio Signal Processing?

While Fourier analysis applies to everything from heat conduction to quantum mechanics, audio processing is the ideal domain for an IB Extended Essay. The data is easy to obtain — you can record sounds with a smartphone. The results are intuitive — you can literally hear the difference when you filter a signal. And the frequency ranges involved (20 Hz to 20,000 Hz for human hearing) are tangible and meaningful. Students who work with audio data find themselves genuinely engaged with their research, and that engagement comes through in the writing.

Audio also provides natural complexity. A single musical note from a piano contains a fundamental frequency plus overtones (harmonics) that give the instrument its characteristic timbre. A chord combines multiple fundamentals. A full recording combines multiple instruments, each with their own harmonic structure, plus environmental noise. Decomposing this complexity into individual frequency components using Fourier analysis is both mathematically sophisticated and practically illuminating.

Recommended Research Questions

Primary recommendation: "How can Fourier analysis be used to identify and isolate individual instruments in a polyphonic audio recording?"

This question works because it requires you to demonstrate understanding of Fourier theory, design and execute an experimental methodology, and critically evaluate the limitations of your approach. The word "how" invites a process-oriented investigation, while the specificity of "polyphonic audio" gives you a clear scope.

Alternative research questions:

  • "How does the window size in a Short-Time Fourier Transform affect the trade-off between time resolution and frequency resolution in audio analysis?"
  • "To what extent can Fourier-based filtering remove background noise from a speech recording while preserving intelligibility?"
  • "How do the harmonic structures of different musical instruments compare when analyzed using the Discrete Fourier Transform, and how does this relate to perceived timbre?"

Key Concepts You Need to Master

Fourier Series

The starting point is Fourier's insight: any periodic function can be represented as a sum of sine and cosine waves of different frequencies, amplitudes, and phases. For a function f(t) with period T, the Fourier series is f(t) = a_0/2 + sum of (a_n cos(2*pi*n*t/T) + b_n sin(2*pi*n*t/T)) for n = 1 to infinity. The coefficients a_n and b_n are computed using integrals that project f(t) onto each basis function. In your essay, you should derive or clearly explain these coefficient formulas and demonstrate that you understand what "decomposing a signal into frequencies" actually means mathematically.

The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

Real audio data is not a continuous function — it is a sequence of discrete samples taken at regular intervals. The DFT transforms a sequence of N time-domain samples into N frequency-domain coefficients. Each coefficient X[k] represents the amplitude and phase of a specific frequency component. The frequency resolution is determined by the sampling rate divided by N: higher N gives finer frequency resolution but requires more data. Understanding this trade-off is crucial for your experimental design.

The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)

The naive DFT computation requires O(N^2) operations, which is prohibitively slow for real audio signals (a 3-second recording at 44,100 Hz contains 132,300 samples). The FFT algorithm, discovered by Cooley and Tukey in 1965, computes the same result in O(N log N) operations by exploiting symmetries in the DFT matrix. You do not need to implement the FFT from scratch — Python's numpy.fft module provides it — but you should explain the algorithmic insight and why the speedup matters. This demonstrates understanding of computational complexity, which examiners value.

The Sampling Theorem (Nyquist-Shannon)

The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem states that to accurately capture a signal containing frequencies up to f_max Hz, you must sample at a rate of at least 2*f_max samples per second. This is why CD-quality audio uses a 44,100 Hz sampling rate — it captures frequencies up to 22,050 Hz, slightly above the upper limit of human hearing (roughly 20,000 Hz). If you sample too slowly, high-frequency components "fold" into lower frequencies, producing artifacts called aliasing. Discussing this theorem and potentially demonstrating aliasing experimentally strengthens your essay considerably.

Spectrograms

A spectrogram is a visual representation of how the frequency content of a signal changes over time. It is computed using the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT): divide the signal into overlapping windows, compute the FFT of each window, and display the results as a 2D image where the x-axis is time, the y-axis is frequency, and color represents amplitude. Spectrograms are the single most powerful visualization for audio analysis in your essay. They reveal patterns invisible in the raw waveform — you can see individual notes, harmonics, percussion hits, and noise bands at a glance.

Methodology: Your Experimental Approach

I recommend Python with numpy, scipy, and matplotlib as your primary tools, supplemented by Audacity (free, open-source audio editor) for recording and basic audio manipulation.

  1. Record or obtain audio samples: Record individual instruments (piano, guitar, flute) playing single notes and simple melodies. Then record combinations. Use a quiet room and consistent recording distance. Alternatively, use royalty-free audio samples from online libraries — but recording your own demonstrates greater personal engagement.
  2. Compute and visualize FFTs: For each recording, compute the FFT and plot the magnitude spectrum (amplitude vs. frequency). Identify fundamental frequencies and harmonics. Compare the harmonic structure of different instruments playing the same note.
  3. Generate spectrograms: Create STFT spectrograms of your recordings. Experiment with different window sizes (256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096 samples) and document how window size affects the time-frequency trade-off.
  4. Implement frequency filtering: Design and apply bandpass filters to isolate specific frequency ranges. For example, extract the bass line from a recording by keeping only frequencies below 300 Hz. Evaluate the quality of the filtered output — can you hear the isolated instrument clearly?
  5. Attempt instrument separation: For a polyphonic recording (two instruments playing simultaneously), try to separate them using frequency-domain filtering. Document both successes and limitations honestly.
Common pitfall: Many students produce beautiful spectrograms and declare their analysis complete. The spectrogram is a tool, not a result. You must interpret what the spectrogram shows, explain why specific patterns appear, and connect your observations back to Fourier theory. Always ask: "What does this frequency pattern tell us, and why does the mathematics predict it?"

Tools and Software

Your Python toolkit should include:

  • numpy and scipy.fft for FFT computation
  • scipy.signal for windowing functions and filter design
  • matplotlib for plotting spectra and spectrograms
  • librosa (optional) for advanced audio analysis features
  • Audacity for recording, playback, and basic editing

All of these are free and well-documented. Include your Python code in the appendix of your essay — examiners appreciate seeing the actual implementation, and it demonstrates that you wrote the analysis code yourself rather than relying on pre-built tools.

Structuring Your Extended Essay

  1. Introduction (400 words): research question, why Fourier analysis matters, scope of investigation
  2. Mathematical Background (800 words): Fourier series, DFT derivation, FFT algorithm, sampling theorem
  3. Methodology (500 words): recording setup, software tools, experimental design
  4. Results and Analysis (1200 words): frequency spectra, spectrograms, filtering results, instrument comparison
  5. Discussion (700 words): limitations, real-world applications, comparison with professional techniques
  6. Conclusion (400 words): answer the research question, reflect on what Fourier analysis reveals about sound

The strongest Fourier EEs I have seen combine mathematical derivation with hands-on experimentation. Do not just explain the theory — apply it to real audio data and show what it reveals. That combination of theory and practice is what earns top marks.
