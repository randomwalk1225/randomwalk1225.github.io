IGCSE Mathematics: Geometry & Trigonometry

Cambridge IGCSE & Edexcel IGCSE · Updated March 2026 · 30 min read

Geometry and Trigonometry together account for a large portion of every IGCSE Mathematics paper. This guide systematically covers all major topics, from the fundamental angle rules through to vectors, with clear worked examples at every stage.

Exam Board Note

This guide covers Cambridge IGCSE Mathematics (0580) and Edexcel International GCSE (4MA1). Circle theorems and vectors are assessed by both boards. The sine rule, cosine rule, and 3D trigonometry are Extended tier topics.

1. Angles and Shapes

Key Angle Rules

Angles on a straight line sum to $180°$.

Angles at a point sum to $360°$.

Vertically opposite angles are equal.

Alternate angles (Z-angles) between parallel lines are equal.

Co-interior angles (C-angles) between parallel lines sum to $180°$.

Corresponding angles (F-angles) between parallel lines are equal.

Polygons

For a regular polygon with $n$ sides:

$$\text{Sum of interior angles} = (n-2) \times 180°$$

$$\text{Each interior angle} = \frac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n}$$

$$\text{Each exterior angle} = \frac{360°}{n}$$

Worked Example 1.1 — Interior Angles of a Regular Polygon

Find the interior angle of a regular nonagon (9 sides).

Step 1 Sum of interior angles $= (9-2) \times 180° = 7 \times 180° = 1260°$.

Step 2 Each interior angle $= \dfrac{1260°}{9} = 140°$.

2. Pythagoras' Theorem

Pythagoras' Theorem

In a right-angled triangle with hypotenuse $c$ and legs $a, b$:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

Worked Example 2.1 — 3D Pythagoras

A cuboid has dimensions $8\,\text{cm} \times 6\,\text{cm} \times 3\,\text{cm}$. Find the length of the space diagonal.

Step 1 Find the diagonal of the base: $d_{\text{base}} = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{64 + 36} = \sqrt{100} = 10\,\text{cm}$.

Step 2 Use Pythagoras in 3D: the space diagonal $= \sqrt{10^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{100 + 9} = \sqrt{109} \approx 10.44\,\text{cm}$.

3. Right-Angled Trigonometry (SOHCAHTOA)

Trigonometric Ratios

In a right-angled triangle with angle $\theta$, opposite side $O$, adjacent side $A$, and hypotenuse $H$:

$$\sin\theta = \frac{O}{H}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{A}{H}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{O}{A}$$

Worked Example 3.1 — Finding a Side and an Angle

(a) In a right-angled triangle, the hypotenuse is 15 cm and one angle is 34°. Find the side opposite to 34°.

(b) A ladder of length 5 m leans against a wall, with its foot 1.8 m from the wall. Find the angle the ladder makes with the ground.

(a) $O = H \sin\theta = 15 \sin 34° = 15 \times 0.5592 \approx 8.39\,\text{cm}$.

(b) $\cos\theta = \dfrac{A}{H} = \dfrac{1.8}{5} = 0.36$, so $\theta = \cos^{-1}(0.36) \approx 68.9°$.

Angles of Elevation and Depression

The angle of elevation is measured upward from the horizontal. The angle of depression is measured downward from the horizontal. They are alternate angles, so they are equal when two objects are at different heights on the same vertical line.

4. Sine Rule and Cosine Rule

These formulae apply to any triangle, not just right-angled ones. They are on the Cambridge and Edexcel formula sheets, but you must know when to apply each one.

The Sine Rule

$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$

Use when given: two sides and an opposite angle (SSA), or two angles and one side (AAS/ASA).

Rearrange as $\dfrac{\sin A}{a} = \dfrac{\sin B}{b} = \dfrac{\sin C}{c}$ when finding an angle.

The Cosine Rule

$$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A$$

Rearranged to find $A$: $\cos A = \dfrac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc}$

Use when given: three sides (SSS), or two sides and the included angle (SAS).

Area of a Triangle

$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$$

Use when you know two sides and the included angle.

Worked Example 4.1 — Using the Cosine Rule

Triangle $PQR$ has $PQ = 7\,\text{cm}$, $PR = 9\,\text{cm}$, and angle $P = 52°$. Find the length $QR$ and the area of the triangle.

Step 1 $QR^2 = 7^2 + 9^2 - 2(7)(9)\cos 52° = 49 + 81 - 126\cos 52°$

$= 130 - 126 \times 0.6157 = 130 - 77.58 = 52.42$

$QR = \sqrt{52.42} \approx 7.24\,\text{cm}$

Step 2 Area $= \dfrac{1}{2}(7)(9)\sin 52° = 31.5 \times 0.7880 \approx 24.8\,\text{cm}^2$.

Worked Example 4.2 — The Ambiguous Case of the Sine Rule

In triangle $ABC$, $AB = 10\,\text{cm}$, $BC = 8\,\text{cm}$, and angle $A = 38°$. Find all possible values of angle $C$.

Step 1 Apply the sine rule: $\dfrac{\sin C}{AB} = \dfrac{\sin A}{BC}$

$\sin C = \dfrac{10 \sin 38°}{8} = \dfrac{10 \times 0.6157}{8} = 0.7696$

Step 2 Since $\sin C = 0.7696$, there are two possible solutions:

$C_1 = \sin^{-1}(0.7696) \approx 50.3°$ and $C_2 = 180° - 50.3° = 129.7°$

Step 3 Check both: $A + C_1 = 38° + 50.3° = 88.3° < 180°$ (valid). $A + C_2 = 38° + 129.7° = 167.7° < 180°$ (also valid). Both triangles exist.

5. Circle Theorems

Circle theorems are among the most frequently examined topics. You must know all eight main theorems and be able to give reasons in your working.

The Eight Circle Theorems

  • Angle at centre: The angle subtended at the centre is twice the angle subtended at the circumference from the same arc.
  • Angle in a semicircle: The angle in a semicircle (subtended by the diameter) is $90°$.
  • Angles in the same segment: Angles subtended by the same chord at the circumference are equal.
  • Cyclic quadrilateral: Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to $180°$.
  • Tangent perpendicular to radius: A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact.
  • Two tangents from an external point: Tangents from an external point are equal in length.
  • Alternate segment theorem: The angle between a tangent and a chord equals the angle in the alternate segment.
  • Perpendicular from centre to chord: A perpendicular from the centre bisects the chord.

Worked Example 5.1 — Applying Circle Theorems

Points $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ lie on a circle. $O$ is the centre. Angle $BOD = 136°$ and $BD$ is a diameter. Find angle $BAD$.

Step 1 Since $BD$ is a diameter and $A$ is on the circle, angle $BAD = 90°$ (angle in a semicircle).

Alternatively, angle at centre $= 136°$ implies the reflex angle at centre is $360° - 136° = 224°$. Angle $BAD = \dfrac{224°}{2} = 112°$. But here $BD$ being a diameter means the arc containing $A$ is a semicircle, confirming $\angle BAD = 90°$.

Always state your theorem as the reason: "angle in a semicircle."

6. Transformations

You must describe transformations fully, using the key word and all required details.

The Four Transformations

Translation: Describe with a column vector $\binom{x}{y}$. Shape, size, and orientation are preserved.

Rotation: State the angle, direction (clockwise/anti-clockwise), and centre of rotation.

Reflection: State the equation of the mirror line.

Enlargement: State the scale factor and centre of enlargement. For fractional scale factors the image is smaller; negative scale factors produce an inverted image.

Worked Example 6.1 — Describing a Transformation

Triangle $A$ has vertices $(1,1)$, $(3,1)$, $(3,4)$. Triangle $B$ has vertices $(5,3)$, $(9,3)$, $(9,9)$. Describe fully the transformation that maps $A$ onto $B$.

Step 1 Compare corresponding side lengths. $A$ has base $= 2$, $B$ has base $= 4$. Scale factor $= 2$. This is an enlargement.

Step 2 Find the centre. Draw rays through corresponding vertices. Lines through $(1,1) \to (5,3)$ and $(3,1) \to (9,3)$: both pass through $(-3,-1)$.

Result Enlargement, scale factor $2$, centre $(-3, -1)$.

7. Vectors

Vectors have both magnitude and direction. In IGCSE, vectors are written as column vectors or using bold letters ($\mathbf{a}$) or letters with an underline.

Vector Operations

If $\mathbf{a} = \binom{a_1}{a_2}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \binom{b_1}{b_2}$:

$$\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = \binom{a_1+b_1}{a_2+b_2}, \quad k\mathbf{a} = \binom{ka_1}{ka_2}$$

$$|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2}$$

Worked Example 7.1 — Vectors in Geometry (Extended)

$OABC$ is a parallelogram. $\overrightarrow{OA} = \mathbf{a}$ and $\overrightarrow{OC} = \mathbf{c}$. $M$ is the midpoint of $AB$. Express $\overrightarrow{OM}$ in terms of $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{c}$.

Step 1 In the parallelogram, $\overrightarrow{CB} = \overrightarrow{OA} = \mathbf{a}$. So $\overrightarrow{OB} = \overrightarrow{OC} + \overrightarrow{CB} = \mathbf{c} + \mathbf{a}$.

Step 2 $M$ is the midpoint of $AB$, so $\overrightarrow{AM} = \dfrac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{AB}$. Now $\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OA} = (\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{c}$.

Step 3 $\overrightarrow{OM} = \overrightarrow{OA} + \overrightarrow{AM} = \mathbf{a} + \dfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{c}$.

8. Constructions and Loci

Constructions must be done with a ruler and compasses only; all arcs must be left showing. Key constructions include:

  • Perpendicular bisector of a line segment
  • Angle bisector
  • Perpendicular from a point to a line
  • Construction of 60°, 90°, and 45° angles

A locus is the set of all points satisfying a given condition. Common loci:

  • Fixed distance from a point: a circle.
  • Equidistant from two points: perpendicular bisector of the segment joining them.
  • Fixed distance from a line: parallel lines on either side.
  • Equidistant from two lines: angle bisector of the angle between them.

9. Practice Problems

Problem 1

A regular polygon has interior angles of $156°$. How many sides does it have?

Show Solution

Exterior angle $= 180° - 156° = 24°$. Number of sides $= \dfrac{360°}{24°} = 15$.

Problem 2

A right-angled triangle has legs $5\,\text{cm}$ and $12\,\text{cm}$. Find the hypotenuse and the largest acute angle.

Show Solution

Hypotenuse $= \sqrt{5^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{169} = 13\,\text{cm}$.

The largest acute angle is opposite the longest leg (12 cm):

$\tan\theta = \dfrac{12}{5} \Rightarrow \theta = \tan^{-1}(2.4) \approx 67.4°$.

Problem 3

In triangle $XYZ$, $XY = 11\,\text{cm}$, $YZ = 7\,\text{cm}$, $XZ = 9\,\text{cm}$. Find angle $Y$.

Show Solution

Using the cosine rule with $y = XZ = 9$ (opposite $Y$), $x = YZ = 7$, $z = XY = 11$:

$$\cos Y = \frac{x^2 + z^2 - y^2}{2xz} = \frac{49 + 121 - 81}{2 \times 7 \times 11} = \frac{89}{154} \approx 0.5779$$

$Y = \cos^{-1}(0.5779) \approx 54.7°$

Problem 4

$A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ lie on a circle. $AB$ is a diameter. Angle $ACD = 28°$ and angle $ABD = 35°$. Find angle $BDC$.

Show Solution

Since $AB$ is a diameter, angle $ADB = 90°$ (angle in a semicircle).

Angles in the same segment: angle $CAD = $ angle $CBD = 28°$ (both subtend arc $CD$).

In triangle $ABD$: angle $ADB + ABD + DAB = 180°$, so angle $DAB = 180° - 90° - 35° = 55°$.

Angle $BDC$ subtends arc $BC$. Angle $BAC$ also subtends arc $BC$: angle $BAC = $ angle $DAB - $ angle $DAC$. Using angles in the same segment and the given data, angle $BDC = 35°$ (same arc $BC$ as angle $BAC = 35°$). Actually: since angle $ABD = 35°$ and they are in the same segment as arc $AD$, we get angle $ACD = $ angle $ABD = 35°$ only if both subtend the same arc. Given angle $ACD = 28°$: angle $BDC = $ angle $BAC$. In triangle $ABD$: angle $DAB = 55°$. Angle $BDC = $ angle $BAC = $ angle $DAB - $ angle $DAC$. Since angles in same segment: $\angle DAC = \angle DBC = ?$ A complete solution requires the full figure, but the key steps use angle in a semicircle, angles in same segment, and angles in a triangle.

Problem 5

Vector $\mathbf{p} = \binom{3}{-4}$. Find $|\mathbf{p}|$ and the unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{p}$.

Show Solution

$|\mathbf{p}| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$.

Unit vector $= \dfrac{1}{5}\binom{3}{-4} = \binom{0.6}{-0.8}$.

Problem 6

From the top of a cliff 80 m high, the angle of depression to a boat at sea is 23°. Find the horizontal distance from the base of the cliff to the boat.

Show Solution

$\tan 23° = \dfrac{80}{d}$, so $d = \dfrac{80}{\tan 23°} = \dfrac{80}{0.4245} \approx 188.5\,\text{m}$.

Problem 7

Find the area of triangle $ABC$ where $AB = 12\,\text{cm}$, $AC = 9\,\text{cm}$, and angle $A = 62°$. Give your answer to 3 significant figures.

Show Solution

Area $= \dfrac{1}{2} \times 12 \times 9 \times \sin 62° = 54 \times 0.8829 \approx 47.7\,\text{cm}^2$.

Problem 8

Describe fully the transformation that maps the point $(2, 5)$ to $(-5, 2)$.

Show Solution

Check: reflection in the line $y = -x$ maps $(x,y) \to (-y,-x)$: $(2,5) \to (-5,-2)$. Not a match.

Rotation by $90°$ anti-clockwise about origin maps $(x,y) \to (-y,x)$: $(2,5) \to (-5,2)$. Yes!

The transformation is a rotation of 90° anti-clockwise about the origin.
